\begin{abstract}
The \emph{Internet of Value} (IoV) --- the heterogeneous network of chains, bridges, AMMs,
and tokens through which value is moved and held --- has matured to the point that the
dominant marginal risk has shifted from any single venue to the \emph{composite, agentic
context} in which an action is taken: the joint state of route, sentiment, liquidity,
and the policies an autonomous system is willing to commit to. Cross-chain bridges
remain a concentrated source of loss --- industry trackers attribute roughly USD~2B of
theft in the first eight months of 2022 alone to bridge attacks~\cite{chainalysis2022bridges}
and the most recent peer-reviewed systematisation places cumulative losses at
approximately USD~3.1B~\cite{augusto2024sok} --- but they are no longer the only place
where context-blind risk telemetry produces avoidable losses, as the rug-pull, sniper-bot,
and micro-cap-launch literature demonstrates~\cite{cernera2023token}. This paper argues
that the correct primitive for IoV risk is neither another risk API nor a single
detection model but a \textbf{five-engine architecture for agentic, context-aware risk
prediction}: a \emph{prediction engine} that emits point and distributional forecasts of
price, liquidity, volatility, and route health; an \emph{agentic engine} that converts
those forecasts into role-bound, constitutionally-constrained actions in the sense of
Anthropic's constitutional AI specification~\cite{bai2022constitutional}; a
\emph{sentiment-fusion engine} that combines off-chain text streams with on-chain flow
signals using the early-fusion / late-fusion / stacking taxonomy of the financial-NLP
survey literature~\cite{schumaker2009textual,xing2018natural,day2016deep}; a
\emph{Bittensor prediction subnet}~\cite{rao2021bittensor,opentensor2024yuma,
taoshi2024ptn} that decentralises the prediction layer; and an \emph{API risk and
scenario engine} that turns predicted scenarios into pre-committed action programs in
the sense of Monte-Carlo financial-engineering scenario generation~\cite{glasserman2003monte}
and the NIST tabletop-exercise framework~\cite{nist2006sp80084}. We present
\textsc{OmniRisk} as the agentic engine, \textsc{RayaChain} (an Omnichain Fungible Token
deployment on LayerZero~V2~\cite{zarick2024layerzero,layerzero2024oft}) as the data and
execution substrate, and Bittensor's Yuma Consensus~\cite{steeves2022incentivizing}
anchored on Subtensor EVM (chain~ID~945)~\cite{opentensor2024evm} as the verification
substrate. We formalise the route, risk-score, and validator-loss primitives, specify
the miner and validator protocols, situate the design against the cross-chain bridge
security, decentralised-ML, financial-NLP, and tabletop-exercise
literatures~\cite{augusto2024sok,zhang2023sok,wu2025safeguarding,conway2024opml,
ganescu2024trust}, and instrument a fully-public five-hour case study --- the
2026-05-06 \textsc{RYA} liquidity-defence cycle --- in which the scenario engine ran
in production as a rule-based liquidity-support program on a single Solana micro-cap
pool. The case study is honest about its scope: a single 5-hour cycle, on a single
pool, with the agentic engine instantiated as a deterministic policy rather than as
an LLM-mediated one. The LLM-mediated agentic engine and the integrated subnet remain
the next-step build, against the falsifiable hypotheses H1 and H2 stated below.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

\paragraph{The IoV reframe.} The phrase \emph{Internet of Value} has been in industry
circulation for nearly a decade, but it has only recently acquired a precise operational
meaning: a heterogeneous, partially-trusted network in which fungible and non-fungible
value flows across chains, AMMs, and bridges, and in which the marginal risk faced by a
participant is increasingly a property of the \emph{composite context} (route, sentiment,
liquidity, observable agentic policy) rather than of any single venue. Bridge security
remains a first-class concern --- the cumulative bridge-loss tally of approximately
USD~3.1B documented by \cite{augusto2024sok} continues to grow --- but the threat
surface has expanded to micro-cap pool dynamics on Solana and other high-throughput L1s,
where rug pulls, sniper bots, and adverse-selection patterns dominate the risk to a
holder's position regardless of bridge state~\cite{cernera2023token}. A risk primitive
adequate for this regime has to compose four things that today live in separate
literatures: forecast, policy, sentiment, and pre-committed action.

\textsc{OmniRisk} is a live cross-chain risk-intelligence service, and \textsc{RayaChain}
is an actively deploying cross-chain transfer fabric. RayaChain's transfer lanes are
implemented as Omnichain Fungible Tokens (OFTs) on LayerZero~V2, the protocol family
that separates message verification (Decentralised Verifier Networks, DVNs) from message
execution (Executors) and exposes per-application security configuration as a
permissionless surface~\cite{zarick2024layerzero,layerzero2024oft}. As of submission,
RayaChain is operationally integrated on six \emph{testnet} chains --- Ethereum
Sepolia, Arbitrum Sepolia, Base Sepolia, BSC testnet, Polygon Amoy, and Solana
devnet --- with no mainnet deployments to date and an estimated four-to-eight
weeks of pre-launch security and configuration work outstanding before mainnet
cutover (Section~\ref{sec:status}). The next planned addition after mainnet is
the Subtensor EVM (chain~ID~945)~\cite{opentensor2024evm}. The paper is
deliberately written against this honest snapshot: the verification-architecture
thesis stands or falls on its design, and we do not lean on production-scale
deployment claims that the engineering ground truth would not support.

The motivation for the next planned lane is not capacity but \emph{verifiability}. Routing
value across heterogeneous chains exposes participants to a class of risks that survey
work has documented in detail: design flaws in bridge architectures map onto a stable
catalogue of attack surfaces~\cite{zhang2023sok}, attack-transaction analyses confirm
that the same patterns recur across dozens of incidents~\cite{wu2025safeguarding}, and
losses concentrate in the bridge layer to a degree that no other DeFi primitive
matches~\cite{chainalysis2022bridges,augusto2024sok}. Existing risk tools react to this
by emitting black-box risk scores: a downstream contract or wallet must trust the
operator's API, with no cryptographic or economic mechanism that constrains the
operator to be honest. This is the same primitive failure that gave rise to the
oracle-design literature in the early DeFi era, and we argue it now calls for a
verification layer that is itself decentralised.

\paragraph{Research question.} The paper is organised around one question:
\begin{quote}
\emph{Can risk prediction for the Internet of Value be made simultaneously
agentic, context-aware, and verifiable, by composing five engines (prediction,
agentic, sentiment fusion, decentralised prediction subnet, scenario) on a
verifiable cross-chain substrate?}
\end{quote}
This is a hypothesis, not a result. The architecture
(Sections~\ref{sec:five-engine}--\ref{sec:integration}), the formal definitions
(Section~\ref{sec:formal}), the evaluation framework (Section~\ref{sec:eval}), and the
single-cycle case study of Section~\ref{sec:case-study} are the apparatus needed to
answer it; the broader answer is deferred to follow-up empirical work. We make the
hypothesis falsifiable by stating two specific research questions and two hypotheses in
Section~\ref{sec:rq}.

\paragraph{Novelty claim.} The strongest novelty is the \textbf{five-engine
composition}, of which the verifiable cross-chain bridge architecture of
\cite{augusto2024sok,zhang2023sok,wu2025safeguarding} is the data substrate but not the
contribution: route-level risk outputs are produced by a heterogeneous population of
miners on a Bittensor prediction subnet, fused with a context-aware sentiment-fusion
engine, evaluated by an agentic engine under constitutional-AI-style action constraints
\cite{bai2022constitutional}, and converted into pre-committed action programs by a
scenario engine in the sense of \cite{glasserman2003monte,nist2006sp80084}, with
verification of intermediate signals economically enforced by decentralised
validators~\cite{rao2021bittensor,steeves2022incentivizing,opentensor2024yuma}. To our
knowledge no published system composes the five together; we situate it precisely
against prior work in Section~\ref{sec:related} and specify the composition in
Section~\ref{sec:five-engine}.

\paragraph{Contributions.} Concretely, the paper makes six contributions.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{A five-engine architecture for agentic IoV risk prediction}: prediction,
        agentic, sentiment fusion, Bittensor prediction subnet, and API risk + scenario,
        composed into a single coherent dataflow (Section~\ref{sec:five-engine}).
  \item \textbf{An architectural separation of concerns}: OmniRisk as the agentic engine,
        RayaChain as data and execution, Bittensor as the verification substrate, with
        each engine mapped onto an existing or planned subsystem
        (Section~\ref{sec:arch}).
  \item \textbf{A formalisation of the cross-chain risk-scoring task} in terms of
        routes $q$, the risk-score function $s(q,t)$, and a validator loss $L_i$ that
        decomposes into accuracy, consistency, and calibration terms
        (Section~\ref{sec:formal}).
  \item \textbf{A miner protocol specification} for thin OmniRisk-API wrappers that meet
        the determinism and structured-reasoning requirements of Yuma
        scoring~\cite{opentensor2024yuma} (Section~\ref{sec:miner}).
  \item \textbf{A validator-side scoring rule} that evaluates miner outputs against
        ground-truth realised cross-chain risk events, expressed in the Yuma weight-vector
        format with precise definitions of \emph{realised loss}, \emph{material chain-state
        change}, and \emph{confidence calibration} (Section~\ref{sec:validator}).
  \item \textbf{An instrumented case study} --- the 2026-05-06 RYA liquidity-defence
        cycle --- in which the scenario engine ran in production as a rule-based
        Solana micro-cap defence against the threat patterns documented
        in~\cite{cernera2023token}; thirty buys totalling 3.0~SOL over five hours,
        with full decision-log timestamps, prices, and on-chain transaction signatures
        (Section~\ref{sec:case-study}). The companion operator-side narrative is
        published as~\cite{omnirisk2026defending}.
\end{enumerate}

This is a working paper. We are explicit throughout about which components are deployed
today, which are in active integration, and which are design proposals (see
Table~\ref{tab:status} in Section~\ref{sec:status}).

\section{Problem Definition}\label{sec:problem}

\paragraph{Unified risk visibility across chains is missing.} A token's risk profile is
not a property of any single chain. The same OFT can have safe liquidity on Ethereum
and dangerously thin liquidity on a destination rollup; a user routing through it will
realise the worse of the two. Empirical AMM-level evidence shows that liquidity is
materially mis-allocated across L1 and L2s relative to LP-return
equilibrium~\cite{gogol2024liquidity}, which means \emph{route choice} --- not just
token choice --- is a first-order risk decision.

\paragraph{Real-time route risk awareness is missing.} The bridge security literature
documents 49~attack incidents on cross-chain bridges between June~2021 and
September~2024~\cite{wu2025safeguarding}, and a security/privacy taxonomy spanning
57~interoperability solutions identifies recurring failure modes across them
all~\cite{augusto2024sok}. Yet at the moment a user submits a transfer, they typically
have no signal that the lane they are about to use shares its design with a recently
exploited one.

\paragraph{Verifiable intelligence is missing.} Existing route-risk APIs are
\emph{black-box}: a downstream contract must trust that the API operator scored honestly.
Recent work on optimistic and zero-knowledge ML on
blockchain~\cite{conway2024opml,ganescu2024trust} and on multichain ML-based fraud
detection~\cite{palaiokrassas2023multichain} addresses parts of this gap, but no system
that we are aware of combines (a)~live cross-chain risk scoring with (b)~a decentralised
verification layer for the scores themselves.

\section{A Five-Engine Architecture for Agentic IoV Risk}\label{sec:five-engine}

The IoV reframe of Section~\ref{sec:intro} forces a corresponding reframe of the risk
primitive. A point risk score on a single route, in isolation, is no longer the right
output: a participant has to decide \emph{what to do} given (i)~a forecast over a set
of correlated quantities, (ii)~a constrained action policy, (iii)~the off-chain text
context that drives short-horizon flows, (iv)~an opinion about the credibility of the
forecast, and (v)~a pre-committed program from scenario to action. We name these five
sub-tasks as engines and require that they compose. Figure~\ref{fig:five-engine}
gives the block diagram; the rest of this section defines each engine's inputs,
outputs, and external citation, and the existing system in Section~\ref{sec:arch}
identifies which engine each existing component implements.

\subsection{Prediction engine}\label{sec:engine:prediction}

The prediction engine produces \emph{point and distributional forecasts} of the
quantities that drive route- and pool-level risk: price, liquidity depth, volatility,
trade-volume distribution, and route-health scalars (latency, success rate, p99
attestation time). It is the input to every other engine.

\paragraph{Inputs.} On-chain state: chain heads, AMM reserves, OFT lane configuration
(\texttt{UlnConfig}, \texttt{ExecutorConfig}~\cite{zarick2024layerzero}), recent
\texttt{OFTSent}/\texttt{OFTReceived} events, BridgeGuard incident-cluster
proximity~\cite{wu2025safeguarding}, micro-cap pool dynamics in the sense
of~\cite{cernera2023token}.

\paragraph{Outputs.} For each predicted quantity, a tuple $(\hat{y}, \hat{\sigma},
\Delta)$: point estimate, predictive standard deviation, horizon. The route-risk
score $s(q,t)$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:risk} is one specific projection.

\paragraph{Reference instantiations.} Domain-specific financial language models such
as FinGPT~\cite{yang2023fingpt} and BloombergGPT~\cite{wu2023bloomberggpt} are
reference instantiations for the text-conditioned components of the prediction
engine; the engine itself is model-agnostic.

\subsection{Bittensor prediction subnet}\label{sec:engine:subnet}

The prediction engine is decentralised by exposing it as a Bittensor subnet, in
which a heterogeneous population of miners produce forecasts and validators score
them against realised outcomes under Yuma Consensus~\cite{rao2021bittensor,
steeves2022incentivizing,opentensor2024yuma}. This is the verifiable analogue of
running the prediction engine inside a single operator. Section~\ref{sec:arch:bittensor}
specifies the Yuma mechanics that compose with the validator loss of
Section~\ref{sec:formal:vloss}.

\paragraph{TAO-incentivised distributed prediction differs from centralised
modelling.} A centralised model has one objective and one bias surface; a TAO-incentivised
distributed prediction subnet has a stake-weighted ensemble that is economically
forced toward consensus on realised outcomes and is bonded to penalise miners who
deviate from consensus on materially-unchanged inputs. The closest live precedent is
Taoshi's Subnet 8 (Proprietary Trading Network)~\cite{taoshi2024ptn}, which
incentivises distributed price-prediction signals. The OmniRisk subnet specialises
that template to cross-chain route- and pool-level risk; the validator scoring rule
of Section~\ref{sec:validator} is task-specific (realised cross-chain events rather
than realised price returns), but the Yuma plumbing is identical. Section~\ref{sec:arch:bittensor}
gives the equations.

\subsection{Sentiment-fusion engine}\label{sec:engine:sentiment}

A risk primitive for the IoV cannot ignore off-chain context. The
sentiment-fusion engine produces a real-valued (or distribution-valued) sentiment
signal per asset and per route, fused from a multimodal feature set: text streams
(news, public chat, posts), on-chain signals (transfers, bridge flows, governance
events), and grey-literature feeds (Birdeye, DexScreener listings, Pump.fun trends).

\paragraph{Fusion strategy.} The financial-NLP survey
literature~\cite{schumaker2009textual,xing2018natural,day2016deep} and the
contemporary multimodal-sentiment surveys~\cite{lai2023multimodal,gandhi2023multimodal}
distinguish three canonical strategies: \emph{early fusion} (concatenate raw features
and feed to a single model), \emph{late fusion} (run modality-specific models and
combine their outputs), and \emph{stacking} (a learned meta-model over the late-fusion
outputs). \cite{gandhi2023multimodal} compares where each strategy wins: early fusion
captures cross-modal correlation but bloats dimensionality and is brittle to missing
modalities; late fusion is modality-robust but loses fine-grained interaction; stacking
with a learned meta-model usually dominates when per-modality signals are heterogeneous
in noise floor and timescale. The engine is fusion-strategy-agnostic by construction;
the live OmniRisk instantiation uses late fusion with stacking, on the grounds that
text models and AMM-flow models have very different latency profiles and combining
them at the score-aggregation layer is more robust than at the feature
layer~\cite{gandhi2023multimodal,zhang2025sentistack}.

\paragraph{Verification.} Sentiment outputs are scored on the same Bittensor subnet
as prediction outputs, with the realised-event channel supplying ground truth: a
miner whose sentiment-fusion model produces a high adverse-sentiment score on a
route that subsequently realises an $E_1$--$E_4$ event (Section~\ref{sec:formal:realised})
is rewarded, and one whose score is non-informative is clipped via
Eq.~\eqref{eq:yuma-consensus}.

\subsection{Agentic engine}\label{sec:engine:agentic}

The agentic engine is the LLM-mediated decision-and-action layer. Given (forecast,
sentiment, context), it selects an action from a constrained menu and emits a
structured response. The constraint surface is the \emph{constitution} in the
Anthropic constitutional-AI sense~\cite{bai2022constitutional}: a written principle
list, rather than a free-form goal, against which every candidate action is checked.

\paragraph{Operational stance.} OmniRisk's agentic engine is paused-by-default. Live
flips and daily-cap raises require board sign-off recorded in the issue thread; the
engine cannot expand its own action surface. This stance is a deliberate
choice: the cost of an unconstrained agentic engine acting on micro-cap pool
dynamics is asymmetric, and constitutional-AI-style training without an external
governance gate is insufficient to bound that cost. The agentic engine therefore
sits inside a thin governance shell that exposes \emph{policy proposal} as the
primary action; \emph{policy execution} requires a separate human approval at the
boundary of cap-raise and live-flip.

\paragraph{Role contracts.} OmniRisk specifies one constitution per role
(Trader, Broadcaster, Risk Officer, etc.); each role's action surface is the
intersection of the engine's general principles with the role's specific
restrictions (e.g.\ ``buy-only, never sell'' for the Trader role of the case study
in Section~\ref{sec:case-study}).

\subsection{API risk + scenario engine}\label{sec:engine:scenario}

The scenario engine converts a forecast distribution into a pre-committed action
program of the form ``if scenario~$\Sigma$ realises during window~$W$, execute
program~$P$''. This is the operational content of \emph{context-aware} risk
prediction in the sense of this paper: the system is not just predicting a
quantity, it is committing to an action program whose triggers and bounds are
declared in advance.

\paragraph{Reference frameworks.} Three literatures contribute. Algorithmic
trading and derivatives risk management ground the scenario-generation primitive in
Monte-Carlo financial engineering~\cite{glasserman2003monte}: a scenario is a
sample path under a stochastic model, and a pre-committed action is a stopping rule
on that path. Insurance underwriting contributes the \emph{trigger and reserve}
structure (a trigger condition fires; a reserve is committed against it; a release
condition closes the reserve). Cybersecurity tabletop frameworks --- canonically the
NIST guide to test, training, and exercise programs~\cite{nist2006sp80084} ---
contribute the \emph{precondition, trigger, action, post-condition} structure of a
playbook, which the scenario engine adopts directly: every program is a typed
record with declared preconditions, trigger predicates, action sequence, and
post-conditions.

\paragraph{Bridge to OmniRisk's API surface.} The current OmniRisk API surface
already exposes route-level risk, route-health, and broadcast-publication
endpoints. The scenario engine is the smallest delta: a registry of declared
programs, a trigger evaluator that polls the prediction engine's distributional
outputs against each program's predicates, and a constrained execution path that
routes accepted actions through the Transfer Engine (Section~\ref{sec:arch:rayachain})
or, for the case study of Section~\ref{sec:case-study}, through Jupiter on
Solana. We expand on this delta in Section~\ref{sec:arch:scenario}.

\subsection{Composition}\label{sec:engine:composition}

The engines compose as follows: the prediction engine emits forecasts; the Bittensor
prediction subnet decentralises and verifies them; the sentiment-fusion engine
augments the forecasts with off-chain context; the agentic engine reads the
augmented forecast and proposes an action under role-bound constraints; the
scenario engine converts the proposal into a pre-committed program and dispatches
it to the appropriate execution venue. Verification anchors are at every boundary:
the prediction subnet's outputs are committed on Subtensor EVM
(Section~\ref{sec:subtensor}); the sentiment-fusion engine's outputs are scored on
the same subnet; the agentic engine's actions are recorded on the issue-thread
audit trail (the human governance gate); the scenario engine's executions are
on-chain transactions whose signatures are themselves the verifiable record. The
case study in Section~\ref{sec:case-study} runs an instance of this dataflow with
the agentic engine instantiated as a deterministic policy and the prediction
subnet instantiated as a single rule-based predictor; the LLM-mediated agentic
engine and the live Bittensor subnet are the next-step build.

\subsection{Strategy: five separable engines, not a composite agent}\label{sec:engine:strategy}

Two structurally distinct compositions are available: \emph{five separable engines
behind a shared ingestion and observability fabric}, or \emph{a single composite
agent that exposes the same five capabilities internally}. The architecture and the
roadmap of this paper take the former. We make the choice explicit because the
framing of the contribution depends on it.

\paragraph{Why five separable engines.} A composite agent collapses three properties
the architecture cannot give up. (i)~\emph{Composability of evidence}: institutional
buyers, the API contract, and this paper all describe risk as the sum of
independently-sourced signals, and a composite agent hides that seam. (ii)~\emph{Governance
isolation}: the agentic engine of Section~\ref{sec:engine:agentic} is paused-by-default
with board sign-off, and folding the other four engines into the same process either
over-restricts them or makes the gate harder to audit. (iii)~\emph{Blast radius and
vendor flexibility}: a sentiment-source outage, an LLM swap, or a Bittensor dead-end
must not take prediction or API risk down with it. The shared fabric (a uniform
ingestion path, the signal schema below, an evidence store, and a single
observability surface) is what gives the composition its \emph{coherence} without the
coupling.

\paragraph{Three contracts the fabric enforces.} Separability would be cosmetic
without invariants every engine adheres to:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Signal schema.} Every engine emits records of the form
        \texttt{\{signal\_id, token, chain, score, confidence, evidence[],
        inputs\_hash, model\_version, ts\}}; evidence from one engine is addable to
        another's record without renormalisation.
  \item \textbf{Reproducibility.} \texttt{inputs\_hash}~$+$~\texttt{model\_version} plus the
        logged input bundle must regenerate the same score; this is enforced as a
        CI gate.
  \item \textbf{Governance hook.} The agentic engine is the only consumer of the
        action-emit interface, and that interface is wrapped by the
        paused-by-default + board-approval gate. Composite agents cannot bypass it,
        because they cannot exist; the gate is enforced at the seam.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Build-order implications.} Under this composition the shared fabric is
the only hard prerequisite; API risk + scenario, prediction, and sentiment fusion
are independent and parallelisable once the fabric exists. The Bittensor prediction
subnet of Section~\ref{sec:engine:subnet} is a \emph{killable} dependency of the
prediction engine: if subnet signal-to-noise is poor, the prediction engine
continues without it. The agentic engine is the only true integrator and ships
last, because it consumes the other three as evidence inputs and inherits the
governance gate. A 90-day build window therefore sequences (D0--D20) shared fabric
plus API risk + scenario, (D15--D45) prediction, (D30--D60) sentiment fusion,
(D45--D75) Bittensor research spike with an explicit go/kill decision, and
(D60--D90) agentic engine; if a week is lost the order in which engines drop is
Bittensor first, sentiment fusion second, on the grounds that neither is on the
critical path to a buyer-shippable product. None of these dates is a commitment of
this paper; the sequencing is what justifies the paper's framing of the agentic
engine as the \emph{integrator} rather than as the cross-cutting engine.

\paragraph{Out-of-scope, by design.} The five-engine framing carries a corresponding
list of capabilities the architecture explicitly does not provide. We name them so
the reader can audit later versions of this work against the same boundary:
\emph{(i)~no sell-side execution} (buy-only across the protocol; the agentic engine
cannot emit a sell action); \emph{(ii)~no human-impersonation jitter, social
spoofing, or detection-evasion behaviour}, ever; \emph{(iii)~no
prediction-fires-trades shortcut} --- the prediction engine never reaches the
executor, and only the agentic engine, behind the governance gate, can propose
actions; \emph{(iv)~no custodial wallets or fund-holding flows}; \emph{(v)~no
closed, non-reproducible signals} --- if a signal cannot be replayed from logged
inputs and produce the same score, it does not ship; \emph{(vi)~no removal of the
agentic gate ``for performance''} --- the gate is the product, not overhead. These
boundaries are the invariants the rest of the paper assumes.

\section{Research Questions and Hypotheses}\label{sec:rq}

We make the architectural claim of Section~\ref{sec:intro} falsifiable by reducing it to
two research questions and two hypotheses. Each is operationalised by a quantity that the
evaluation framework of Section~\ref{sec:eval} produces.

\paragraph{RQ1.} Does cross-chain route-level telemetry --- the joint state of source-chain
liquidity, destination-chain liquidity, and the OFT lane configuration --- improve risk
prediction relative to single-chain risk features for the same route?

\paragraph{RQ2.} Can decentralised validators reliably distinguish calibrated risk
outputs from uncalibrated or adversarial miner outputs over repeated replay evaluation?
That is: under the validator scoring rule of Section~\ref{sec:validator}, does the Yuma
emission share converge onto miners whose realised-event accuracy and confidence
calibration are independently good?

\paragraph{H1.} Cross-chain liquidity-fragmentation features improve the early-detection
lead time (number of blocks between threshold crossing and the realised event) for
adverse route events, relative to a single-chain liquidity-only model. This hypothesis is
falsified if the lead-time distribution under the cross-chain feature set is statistically
indistinguishable from the lead-time distribution under the single-chain baseline.

\paragraph{H2.} Confidence-calibrated miners receive monotonically higher validator
weights than uncalibrated miners over repeated replay evaluation. That is: holding the
realised-event accuracy term constant, miners whose Brier score on $(s, c)$ is lower
must achieve a strictly higher Yuma reward share. This hypothesis is falsified if the
correlation is non-significant or has the wrong sign.

The deferred empirical paper will report on RQ1 and RQ2 directly and will accept or reject
H1 and H2 against the baselines specified in Section~\ref{sec:eval:baselines}.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

We organise the literature into six sub-areas, distinguish what each contributes, and
identify the gap that this paper addresses.

\subsection{Cross-chain bridge security surveys}\label{sec:related:bridge-sok}

Two systematisations frame the bridge-security landscape. \cite{augusto2024sok} is the
peer-reviewed SoK at IEEE S\&P 2024: it surveys 212 documents (58 peer-reviewed and 154
grey), enumerates a security/privacy taxonomy across 57 interoperability solutions, and
reports cumulative cross-chain hack losses of approximately USD~3.1B. \cite{zhang2023sok}
provides a complementary attack-surface taxonomy organised by bridge architecture
(lock-and-mint, burn-and-mint, atomic swap, light-client) and identifies recurring
defence gaps. Both are descriptive; neither proposes a verifiable risk-scoring primitive.

\subsection{Bridge attack-detection systems}\label{sec:related:bridge-detection}

\cite{wu2025safeguarding} introduces \textsc{BridgeGuard}, a two-stage graph-mining
detector that combines a global graph view of cross-chain transactions with a local
motif-extraction step. The labelled corpus comprises 203 attack transactions across
49 cross-chain bridge incidents (June~2021 -- September~2024) against 40{,}000 normal
cross-chain transactions, an empirical class-imbalance ratio of approximately
$1{:}197$; the authors report a recall improvement of 36.32 percentage points over
prior state-of-the-art tools and the ability to flag previously unseen attack
transactions~\cite{wu2025safeguarding}. \textsc{BridgeGuard} is the closest existing
analogue to one component of OmniRisk's realised-event channel and we use its labelled
corpus as the canonical $E4$ exploit-cluster label source in
Section~\ref{sec:formal:realised}. Two differences from our work are essential:
(i)~it is a single-operator detector with no verification layer over its outputs, and
(ii)~it is retrospective event-detection rather than ex-ante route-risk prediction.
A consequence for our evaluation framework (Section~\ref{sec:eval}) is that the
$1{:}197$ imbalance is the natural prior for the realised-event channel, which forces
the baselines of Section~\ref{sec:eval:baselines} to be evaluated on imbalance-aware
metrics (precision-recall AUC, not just ROC-AUC) and motivates threshold selection
that prioritises recall on the rare positive class.

\subsection{Multichain fraud detection}\label{sec:related:multichain}

\cite{palaiokrassas2023multichain} releases a labelled multichain fraud-detection dataset
spanning 23 DeFi protocols and 12 chains, with baseline gradient-boosted and deep
classifiers. The contribution is the dataset and baselines, not a route-level risk model
or a verification mechanism. We adopt their multichain framing and note that route-level
telemetry (the OFT lane and its DVN configuration) is an additional feature class not
present in their corpus.

\subsection{Decentralised ML verification}\label{sec:related:dml}

Two complementary verification primitives have appeared in 2024.
\cite{conway2024opml} (\textsc{opML}) verifies off-chain ML inference via an
optimistic-rollup-style fraud-proof challenge game on chain. \cite{ganescu2024trust}
(\textsc{snarkGPT}) verifies transformer inference via zero-knowledge succinct
arguments. Both are general-purpose ML-verification primitives; neither targets
cross-chain risk scoring specifically and neither relies on an economic
validator-quorum mechanism. Bittensor's Yuma consensus is a third verification
primitive --- an economic-validator-quorum mechanism --- and our contribution is to apply
it to the cross-chain risk-scoring task.

\subsection{Oracle and data-verification mechanisms}\label{sec:related:oracle}

The broader DeFi systematisation~\cite{werner2022sok} catalogues data-feed and oracle
mechanisms as a core DeFi primitive: trusted-feeder oracles, Schelling-point oracles, and
zk-attested feeds. The route-risk primitive proposed in this paper is structurally a
\emph{decentralised attestation feed}: it makes a claim of the form ``route $q$ has risk
$s$ at time $t$'', and that claim is meant to be consumed by downstream contracts. The
gap is that existing oracle taxonomies cover \emph{prices} (sourced from venues) and
\emph{external events} (sourced from APIs), but not \emph{model outputs} produced by an
agentic risk pipeline. The Bittensor verification layer of Section~\ref{sec:arch:bittensor}
is the first economic-validator-quorum primitive deployed at production scale that
naturally accommodates model-output attestations of this form.

\subsection{Bittensor incentive mechanisms}\label{sec:related:bittensor}

The Bittensor incentive mechanism is specified canonically in
\cite{rao2021bittensor} and analysed formally in \cite{steeves2022incentivizing}, which
proves collusion-resistance up to coalitions controlling less than 50\% of stake-weighted
rank. The current production specification is documented in
\cite{opentensor2024yuma}, with the Subtensor EVM execution environment in
\cite{opentensor2024evm}. \cite{lui2025bittensor} is the most recent critical empirical
analysis: it documents real centralisation pressure under the dynamic-TAO regime and
should be read alongside the formal collusion-resistance bound rather than as a
contradiction of it. We treat the formal bound as a property of the algorithm and the
empirical findings as a property of the deployed network's stake distribution; both
matter for the security analysis of Section~\ref{sec:security}.

\subsection{Where the gap is}\label{sec:related:gap}

Existing work covers (a)~bridge security descriptively, (b)~retrospective attack
detection, (c)~multichain fraud-detection datasets, (d)~general-purpose ML-verification
primitives, (e)~oracle taxonomies for prices and events, and (f)~the Bittensor incentive
mechanism in isolation. No published system simultaneously
(i)~scores route-level cross-chain risk in real time,
(ii)~submits those scores to a decentralised validator quorum that scores them against
realised events, and
(iii)~anchors the resulting consensus on chain so it can be consumed verifiably by
downstream contracts. That intersection is what this paper proposes.

\subsection{OmniRisk --- Agentic Engine}\label{sec:arch:omnirisk}

OmniRisk is the deployed instance of the \emph{agentic engine} of
Section~\ref{sec:engine:agentic}: a control loop that ingests chain state, selects which
downstream tools (DEX quote APIs, on-chain analytics endpoints, historical incident
databases, the prediction-subnet endpoint of Section~\ref{sec:arch:bittensor}, and the
sentiment-fusion endpoint of Section~\ref{sec:arch:sentiment}) to consult, and emits a
structured response with an explicit confidence and a chain-of-evidence. The agentic
framing is contrasted with \emph{static} analytics --- precomputed dashboards, fixed
risk-scoring formulas --- which do not adapt to the question being asked.

The engine's action surface is bounded by a role-specific constitution in the sense of
\cite{bai2022constitutional}: every role declares its principles (e.g.\ ``buy-only'',
``never use the treasury or deployer wallet'', ``board sign-off required for cap raises
and live flips'') as a checked precondition rather than as a soft prompt. The engine
proposes; a human approval at a separate governance boundary executes. This is what
makes the engine \emph{paused-by-default}: in steady state, OmniRisk emits a continuous
stream of action proposals into an issue thread, and the only proposals that translate
into on-chain transactions are those that pass the role contract and the standing
authorisation. Section~\ref{sec:case-study} shows this loop running in production with
the agentic engine instantiated as a deterministic policy under the Trader role
contract.

\subsection{Sentiment-fusion engine}\label{sec:arch:sentiment}

The sentiment-fusion engine of Section~\ref{sec:engine:sentiment} is implemented as a
late-fusion stack with three modality inputs and a stacked meta-model.

\paragraph{Modality 1 --- text.} Public posts, news headlines, and topic-keyed chat
streams. Per-modality classifiers map text windows to per-asset and per-route sentiment
scalars in $[-1, 1]$. Reference instantiations are surveyed
in~\cite{schumaker2009textual,xing2018natural,day2016deep}; the OmniRisk live deployment
uses a fine-tuned encoder-only model whose details are out of scope for this paper.

\paragraph{Modality 2 --- on-chain.} Transfer-volume distribution, bridge-flow
asymmetry, governance events, and lane-configuration mutations
(Section~\ref{sec:formal:material}). The on-chain modality contributes both as a
sentiment input (transfer-volume asymmetry is a known sentiment proxy) and as a
ground-truth channel against which the text modality is calibrated.

\paragraph{Modality 3 --- grey literature.} For Solana micro-cap pools in particular,
public dashboards (Birdeye~\cite{birdeye2026docs}), DEX listings
(DexScreener~\cite{dexscreener2026docs}), and meme-coin launch trackers
(Pump.fun~\cite{pumpfun2026site}) are the dominant attention surface and are
functionally part of the sentiment input even though they are not peer-reviewed
sources. We treat them as grey-literature data feeds: ingested through scraped APIs,
normalised to a per-asset attention scalar, and combined with the text modality at
the late-fusion stage. Three explicit caveats apply. (i)~The feeds are unmoderated and
vulnerable to wash-trading and pump-and-dump distortion. (ii)~There is no labelled
ground truth: in particular, no peer-reviewed treatment of Birdeye, DexScreener, or
Pump.fun feeds as sentiment proxies has been published, so the calibration of any
classifier downstream of them must be done against external realised-event labels
rather than internal-consistency labels. (iii)~The Pump.fun population (memecoins) is
fat-tailed enough that off-the-shelf sentiment-classifier confidence scores are poorly
calibrated; the sentiment-fusion engine therefore down-weights Pump.fun under high
volatility-of-volatility regimes. The recommendation, consistent with the contemporary
multimodal-sentiment surveys~\cite{lai2023multimodal,gandhi2023multimodal}, is to treat
these feeds as \emph{noisy weak-supervision signals} and combine them via late fusion
or stacking with stronger on-chain primitives, rather than dropping them naively into
early-fusion concatenation --- exactly the failure mode \cite{gandhi2023multimodal} warn
against when modality noise levels are unequal. The case study in
Section~\ref{sec:case-study} relies on this modality to detect the escalation in
adverse sentiment that preceded the 2026-05-06 dip.

\paragraph{Worked-example reference.} Zhang, Jiang, and Lu's 2025 \emph{SentiStack}
network~\cite{zhang2025sentistack} is the clearest contemporary published instance of
the on-chain~$\leftrightarrow$~off-chain fusion pattern this engine implements:
financial-news and social-media sentiment encoded by DeepSeek are fused both early
(concatenated features) and late (per-modality LSTM stacked through a Ridge
meta-learner) with macro and market indicators for Bitcoin forecasting; the authors
explicitly argue that sentiment carries information pure market data cannot reproduce,
particularly across regime breaks. Our engine adopts the same late-fusion + stacking
discipline; the difference is that the OmniRisk routing target is route- and pool-level
risk on the IoV rather than next-window Bitcoin price.

\paragraph{Verification.} Sentiment-fusion engine outputs are scored on the same
Bittensor subnet as prediction-engine outputs, with the realised-event channel of
Section~\ref{sec:formal:realised} supplying ground truth. The Macrocosmos Data
Universe subnet (Bittensor SN13)~\cite{macrocosmos2024datauniverse} is the natural
upstream supplier for the text modality at scale; the sentiment-fusion engine consumes
its output as one input among many, not as a primary source.

\subsection{RayaChain --- Execution and Data Layer}\label{sec:arch:rayachain}

RayaChain is the OFT lane fabric. Each lane is implemented as either a standard OFT
(burn on source, mint on destination) or an OFT Adapter (lock on source, mint on
destination) where a legacy ERC-20 already exists, exactly as specified in the OFT
standard~\cite{layerzero2024oft}. RayaChain inherits LayerZero~V2's permissionless
security configuration: each lane chooses its own DVN set and execution path, which is
the surface OmniRisk monitors when scoring lane-level risk~\cite{zarick2024layerzero}.

The current deployment is six \emph{testnet} integrations: Ethereum Sepolia,
Arbitrum Sepolia, Base Sepolia, BSC testnet, Polygon Amoy, and Solana devnet.
There are zero mainnet deployments as of 2026-05-03; the canonical-origin pointer
in the chain registry still resolves to \texttt{solana-devnet} (LayerZero V2 EID
40168), which has to be repointed to a mainnet endpoint before a production
launch. The Hardhat configuration knows only testnet networks; mainnet RPC URLs
and mainnet EIDs are not yet declared. Section~\ref{sec:status} records this
state and the gap list (Section~\ref{sec:status:gaps}) records the specific
production-readiness items that still have to land. The next planned chain
addition --- sequenced after a mainnet cutover, not before --- is the Subtensor
EVM (chain~ID~945)~\cite{opentensor2024evm}, motivated in
Section~\ref{sec:arch:bittensor}.

\paragraph{Subsystems.} RayaChain is not a monolithic OFT deployment. Three named
modules carry the cross-chain data and execution responsibilities relied on by
OmniRisk and, downstream, by the verification layer of
Section~\ref{sec:arch:bittensor}:
\begin{description}
  \item[Transfer Engine.] An eight-state lifecycle implementing the
        quote~$\to$~build~$\to$~sign~$\to$~send pipeline against LayerZero V2 OFTs,
        with EVM~$\to$~EVM, EVM~$\to$~Solana, and Solana~$\to$~EVM route classes.
        States expose the in-flight window during which OmniRisk's route-risk
        score~$s(q,t)$ (Section~\ref{sec:formal:score}) is most operationally
        relevant.
  \item[Risk Broadcast System.] A hub-spoke broadcast fabric for cross-chain
        risk signals, with topic-keyed feeds, severity levels, and policy
        validation. The EVM path is on-chain; the Solana path is delivered via
        an executor sidecar. Broadcast carries OmniRisk-emitted signals to
        downstream consumers without requiring each consumer to operate its own
        cross-chain listener; the feed itself is one of the surfaces a Bittensor
        subnet can score against (Section~\ref{sec:integration}).
  \item[Route Health System.] Continuous per-lane telemetry over the dimensions
        that drive route selection: p50/p99 attestation latency, destination-side
        liquidity, recent volume, success rate, and a discrete risk grade. The
        feed refreshes on a 15-second cadence and backs both the public Route
        Health UI and the routing decisions surfaced through the Transfer Engine
        quote step.
\end{description}
Section~\ref{sec:status} records which of these subsystems are production-ready,
which are under active hardening, and which are explicit known gaps; the paper
deliberately does not overstate readiness.

\subsection{Bittensor --- Prediction-Engine Subnet and Verification Layer}\label{sec:arch:bittensor}

Bittensor here plays a dual role: as the \emph{decentralised prediction engine} of
Section~\ref{sec:engine:subnet} and as the \emph{verification layer} for both prediction
and sentiment-fusion outputs. \emph{Miners} produce outputs for a specific task and
\emph{validators} score them; rewards are emitted via Yuma Consensus on the Subtensor
blockchain~\cite{rao2021bittensor,steeves2022incentivizing,opentensor2024yuma}. We
summarise the mechanics here because the validator scoring rule of
Section~\ref{sec:validator} composes with them, and because the closest live-2026
precedent --- Taoshi's Subnet 8 (Proprietary Trading Network)~\cite{taoshi2024ptn} ---
gives us a concrete template for what a TAO-incentivised distributed prediction subnet
looks like in production.

\paragraph{Taoshi PTN as a 2026 reference.} PTN's miner schema is a directional-signal
market: miners submit \texttt{LONG}, \texttt{SHORT}, or \texttt{FLAT} orders on
supported pairs and validators run a debt-ledger scoring system that sets weights every
five minutes and pays miners against the prior week's risk-adjusted PnL, with weekly
settlement targeted by midnight Sunday~\cite{taoshi2024ptn}. Three filters bound the
miner population: a 10\% maximum-drawdown elimination cap, a probation regime that gives
miners ranked below the 15th place a 30-day window to recover, and an order-uniqueness
plagiarism detector (\texttt{vali\_objects} analysis stored in
\texttt{validation/miner\_copying.json}) that culls miners simply mirroring others.
Carry costs (10.95\%/3\%/5.25\% per year for crypto/forex/equities at $1\times$
leverage), spread fees, and leverage-scaled slippage are baked into reported PnL, so the
subnet effectively scores live, fee-aware, risk-adjusted trading rather than free-standing
forecasts. As of April 2026, taostats and CoinGecko's dTAO ecosystem review still rank
PTN inside the top ten subnets by Alpha-token market capitalisation
($\sim$USD~47M)~\cite{taoshi2024ptn}, well behind market leaders \textit{$\tau$emplar}
(SN3), \textit{Chutes} (SN64), and \textit{Targon} (SN4), but it remains the only mature
finance-prediction subnet with multi-year operational history.

\paragraph{The 2026 subnet ecosystem.} Two structural changes since the original
Bittensor specification are material to the architecture. First, the February~2025
\emph{dTAO transition} introduced per-subnet Alpha tokens and market-allocated
emissions, so each subnet now carries a market-priced reward that miners voluntarily
accept the carry/slippage drag against; this is qualitatively different from validating
a model on held-out data. Second, the 2026 \emph{Robin~$\tau$ upgrade} doubled subnet
capacity from 128 to 256, which expanded the population of adjacent-but-not-identical
prediction subnets~\cite{taoshi2024ptn}.

\paragraph{Macrocosmos Data Universe (SN13).} The closest neighbour to OmniRisk's remit
is Macrocosmos's \emph{Data Universe} (Bittensor Subnet 13)~\cite{macrocosmos2024datauniverse}:
rather than predicting prices, it produces continuously-refreshed scrapes of X
(Twitter), Reddit, and (planned) YouTube transcripts with engagement metadata --- over
55 billion rows of open-source social data on Hugging Face by July~2025 --- explicitly
chartered as fuel for downstream sentiment-analysis and financial-forecasting consumers.
We have not been able to verify a post-2024 Bittensor subnet whose charter is dedicated
specifically to on-chain risk prediction or IoV-style time-series risk; the surrounding
$\sim$100 subnets are general-purpose LLM, inference, GPU-rental, or data-scraping
markets. The architectural argument for routing prediction through a TAO-incentivised
network rather than through a single in-house model is therefore \emph{not} ``higher
accuracy on a fixed dataset'' --- centralised feature-engineered stacks of the MFNN
type~\cite{long2019deep} routinely beat naive ensembles on benchmark splits --- but
\emph{adversarial diversity and live skin-in-the-game}: PTN's scoring filters
copycats and high-drawdown strategies, and under dTAO every emitted TAO is a
market-priced reward miners voluntarily commit to. For OmniRisk's IoV stack, the
practical implication is that Bittensor signal markets are best treated as one input
modality among many --- not as a drop-in replacement for the in-house engines of
Sections~\ref{sec:arch:omnirisk}--\ref{sec:arch:scenario}.

Let $V$ index subnet validators, $M$ index subnet miners, and $S_i$ denote the stake of
validator $i \in V$. Each validator submits a weight $W_{ij} \in [0,1]$ for each miner
$j$. Yuma computes a benchmark consensus weight per miner as
\begin{equation}
  \overline{W}_j \;=\; \arg\max_{w \in [0,1]} \;
    \Bigl\{\, \textstyle \sum_{i \in V} S_i \cdot \mathbf{1}\!\left[W_{ij} \ge w\right]
    \;\ge\; \kappa \cdot \textstyle \sum_{i \in V} S_i \,\Bigr\}
  \label{eq:yuma-consensus}
\end{equation}
with $\kappa = 0.5$ (the default consensus parameter): $\overline{W}_j$ is the largest
weight that at least the $\kappa$-stake-fraction of validators is willing to assign to
miner $j$. Each validator's submitted weight is then clipped against this benchmark,
$\widehat{W}_{ij} = \min(W_{ij}, \overline{W}_j)$, which bounds the influence of any
single validator above the consensus. Aggregate miner ranking is
$R_j = \sum_{i \in V} S_i \cdot \overline{W}_{ij}$ and the per-epoch miner emission share
is $M_j = R_j / \sum_{k \in M} R_k$~\cite{opentensor2024yuma}. Validators in turn earn
through a bond/dividend matrix $B_{ij}$ that is updated as an EMA of the
stake-weighted clipped weights, so validators are rewarded for being early to the
eventual consensus rather than for following it~\cite{opentensor2024yuma,
steeves2022incentivizing}. The mechanism is provably collusion-resistant up to
coalitions controlling less than 50\% of stake-weighted
rank~\cite{steeves2022incentivizing}.

The implication for a cross-chain risk subnet is concrete: a miner is only economically
viable if its risk scores fall within the consensus envelope of the validator quorum;
miners that deviate are clipped out of the emission, and validators that consistently
support deviating miners lose bonded yield. This is the property we want from a risk
oracle, and it is not provided by any centralised risk API. The Subtensor EVM
(chain~ID~945) gives us an Ethereum-compatible execution environment in which to anchor
commitments to score outputs without leaving the Bittensor trust
domain~\cite{opentensor2024evm}.

\subsection{API risk + scenario engine}\label{sec:arch:scenario}

The scenario engine of Section~\ref{sec:engine:scenario} is the smallest delta over
OmniRisk's existing API surface that turns predicted distributions into pre-committed
action programs. We characterise the as-is surface concretely (because every published
description so far has been at the abstract layer), specify the demands the scenario
engine adds, and quote the smallest-delta build cost in engineer-days.

\paragraph{As-is API surface.} OmniRisk currently runs five distinct HTTP boundaries
plus one bot runtime with file-based control surfaces, all on the operator's machine.

\begin{description}
  \item[Paperclip server (\texttt{:3100/api/\dots}).] The orchestration backbone.
        REST API for company and agent enumeration, issue lifecycle (\texttt{POST/GET/
        PATCH /api/issues/:id}), threaded comments, document attachment, and agent
        heartbeat invocation (\texttt{POST /api/agents/:id/heartbeat/invoke}). Auth
        model: \texttt{local\_trusted} --- localhost requests are treated as the
        board user. Idempotency: issue creation is keyed on caller-provided
        \texttt{idempotencyKey}; heartbeat invocations are fire-and-forget with no
        delivery guarantee. Observability: structured JSON logs;
        no metrics endpoint.
  \item[X bridge (\texttt{:8790}).] Outbound-only to the X v2 API. Surfaces:
        \texttt{POST /tweet} (single tweet, 280-char validated), \texttt{POST /thread}
        (sequential multi-tweet, 500\,ms gap enforced), \texttt{POST /notify}
        (Telegram-compat envelope), \texttt{GET /health}. Auth: shared
        \texttt{X-Bridge-Token}; the bridge signs outbound X requests with OAuth~1.0a
        HMAC-SHA1. No idempotency; rate ceiling $\sim$1{,}500~posts/month on the X
        free tier with no backpressure surface to callers.
  \item[Telegram bridge (\texttt{:8788}).] Bidirectional. Inbound: long-polls the
        Telegram Bot API, creates Paperclip issues assigned to the Personal
        Assistant, then polls issue comments to relay replies (default 5\,s).
        Outbound: \texttt{POST /notify} with \texttt{\{channel,text\}}; channel
        names map to chat IDs via env-var registry.
  \item[Discord bridge (\texttt{:8789}).] Identical outbound contract to Telegram,
        backed by per-channel Discord webhook URLs. Truncates at the Discord
        2{,}000-char hard limit; HTTP 429 retried with exponential backoff.
  \item[Risk-alert poller (client; no inbound port).] Polls
        \texttt{OMNIRISK\_API\_BASE\_URL/v1/dashboard/high-risk-alerts?limit=50}
        on a configurable interval (default 300\,s). Deduplicates by
        \texttt{chain:address}; routes by severity to the Telegram bridge
        (\texttt{critical}/\texttt{high} $\to$ \texttt{alerts}, \texttt{medium}
        $\to$ \texttt{internal}, \texttt{low}/\texttt{info} dropped).
\end{description}

The bot runtime supplies file-based control surfaces, not HTTP: \texttt{bot/state.json}
(cooldown timestamps, daily-spend accumulator, pause flag), \texttt{bot/KILL} (graceful
shutdown sentinel), and \texttt{bot/logs/decisions.jsonl} (append-only structured
decision log). The live-mode gate is a two-flag lock --- \texttt{DRY\_RUN=false} plus
\texttt{LIVE\_CONFIRM=I\_UNDERSTAND\_LIVE\_TRADING} --- both required for real trade
execution. Internally, a \texttt{score()} pure function with deterministic
\texttt{SignedEnvelope} outputs (ed25519 signatures, UUIDv7 identifiers, RFC~8785
JCS canonical form) appends signed signal records to \texttt{output/signals.jsonl};
no HTTP endpoint today accepts or emits these signals.

\paragraph{Scenario-engine demands.} A scenario engine is the conduit between a market
observation and a pre-authorised response. It needs five capabilities the current
surface does not provide:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A \emph{scenario template registry}: a persistent typed store of named
        condition patterns of the form ``liquidity on pool $P$ drops more than $X\%$
        within window $W$''. The bot's tier thresholds are TypeScript constants today
        and cannot be authored, versioned, or queried at runtime.
  \item \emph{Action binding}: each template binds to one or more serialisable
        response actions (``pre-commit $Z$~SOL of buy capacity to tier $T$'', ``post
        alert to channel $C$'', ``invoke agent $A$ with payload $P$''), executable
        without per-event human confirmation in automated mode.
  \item A \emph{generalised two-flag execution gate}: a \texttt{mode} field
        (\texttt{dry\_run}~$|$~\texttt{live}) per scenario execution context, plus a
        per-scenario \texttt{confirmed} bit requiring explicit board sign-off before
        any live action fires. The bot's two-flag pattern today is local env-var
        state inside the bot process and not externally readable.
  \item A \emph{scenario history store} with append-only run records and an HTTP
        query surface (\texttt{GET /scenarios/:id/runs?limit=N\&since=T}). The
        existing \texttt{decisions.jsonl} partially fulfils this for the bot but is
        bot-specific and not queryable over HTTP.
  \item \emph{Agentic composition}: an LLM-mediated authoring path where natural-
        language intent is translated into a validated condition-grammar template,
        plus a Paperclip \texttt{request\_confirmation} interaction to gate live-mode
        activation. None of these flows are wired today.
\end{enumerate}
The trigger-delivery substrate is a sixth requirement: the engine must receive market
events in near real time, where the only inbound market-data path today is the bot's
internal \texttt{priceWatcher.ts} (DexScreener polling, 30\,s cadence). The engine
either subscribes to a shared event emitter exported from the bot or polls its own
adapters.

\paragraph{Smallest-delta build.} Three deployment options, in increasing isolation:
(i)~a \texttt{scenarios/} module inside the bot ($\sim$2 engineer-days; no new
process); (ii)~a Paperclip plugin exposing \texttt{POST/GET /scenarios} and
\texttt{GET /scenarios/:id/runs} with history persisted via the issue/comment system
($\sim$4--5 days); (iii)~a new Node service on \texttt{:8791} with SQLite-backed
templates and history ($\sim$6--8 days). The recommended v0 is (i) plus a thin
in-process HTTP shim on \texttt{:8791} for \texttt{POST /scenarios} and
\texttt{GET /scenarios/:id/runs}, costed at $\sim$3 engineer-days for the rule-based
core (template store, evaluator, history writer, generalised two-flag contract,
\texttt{decisions.jsonl} schema extension) and a fourth day for LLM-mediated authoring
plus the Paperclip \texttt{request\_confirmation} gate. The agentic-authoring layer
ships as a follow-on PR; it does not block the engine from running in dry-run mode on
board-authored templates.

\paragraph{Foundations for the trigger evaluator.} The scenario semantics we want ---
``if scenario~$\Sigma$ is realised in window~$W$ with probability above threshold~$p$,
execute program~$P$ within resource bound~$B$'' --- is the operational form of a
stopping rule on a Monte-Carlo sample path of the predicted joint
distribution~\cite{glasserman2003monte}. The case study of Section~\ref{sec:case-study}
runs the simplest non-trivial scenario program in this class --- a tier-ladder
buy-only liquidity-defence policy --- and demonstrates that the program-registry /
trigger-evaluator / constrained-execution decomposition suffices for that case at
the rule-based level.

\section{OmniRisk--Bittensor Integration Model}\label{sec:integration}

The functional mapping is:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{OmniRisk} generates risk scores and anomaly signals (intelligence).
  \item \textbf{Bittensor validators} act as independent risk-verification nodes
        (verification).
  \item \textbf{RayaChain} contributes the cross-chain flow data and is also one of
        the execution venues whose lanes are being scored (data + execution).
\end{itemize}

A subnet specialised for cross-chain financial intelligence exposes a single task to
its miners: given a route specification (source chain, destination chain, OFT, size,
deadline), return a structured risk score. We do not require miners to all use the same
internal model --- the discipline comes from the validator scoring rule
(Section~\ref{sec:validator}), not from the miner implementation.

\section{Formal Definitions}\label{sec:formal}

We fix notation that the miner specification (Section~\ref{sec:miner}) and the validator
specification (Section~\ref{sec:validator}) both refer to.

\subsection{Route}\label{sec:formal:route}

A \emph{route} is a tuple
\begin{equation}
  q \;=\; (c_s,\; c_d,\; a,\; x,\; t)
  \label{eq:route}
\end{equation}
where $c_s$ is the source chain identifier, $c_d$ the destination chain identifier, $a$
the asset (an OFT in the RayaChain deployment), $x \in \mathbb{R}_{>0}$ the transfer
size denominated in $a$, and $t$ the query time (a wall-clock instant; in
implementation, the most recent finalised block on each of $c_s$ and $c_d$, paired). The
chain identifiers are drawn from the set of RayaChain-active chains
\(
  \mathcal{C} = \{\text{Ethereum},\; \text{Arbitrum},\; \text{Base},\; \text{BNB},\;
  \text{Polygon},\; \text{Optimism},\; \text{Solana}\}
\)
plus the proposed $c = 945$ Subtensor EVM lane (Section~\ref{sec:subtensor}).

\subsection{Risk score}\label{sec:formal:score}

Let $E_{q, t+\Delta} \in \{0, 1\}$ be the indicator of a \emph{realised adverse event}
on route $q$ over the horizon $[t, t + \Delta]$ (the precise event definition is
operationalised in Section~\ref{sec:formal:realised} below). Let $X_{q, t}$ denote the
information set observable on chain at time $t$ that is relevant to $q$ (source/destination
liquidity snapshots, recent OFT throughput, the lane's DVN configuration, recent
incident-cluster proximity).

The route risk score is
\begin{equation}
  s(q, t) \;=\; \Pr\bigl(\, E_{q, t+\Delta} = 1 \;\bigm|\; X_{q, t} \,\bigr) \,.
  \label{eq:risk}
\end{equation}
A miner $i$ emits an estimate $s_i(q, t) \in [0,1]$ of this quantity, together with a
self-reported confidence $c_i(q, t) \in [0,1]$ and a structured reasoning record
$r_i(q, t) \in \mathcal{R}$ (Section~\ref{sec:miner}). Throughout the paper, $\Delta$ is
a subnet-level constant; the deployed value is published with the subnet specification.

\subsection{Realised loss}\label{sec:formal:realised}

The $E_{q, t+\Delta}$ indicator is the disjunction of four observable channels, each
with a precise definition; we explicitly avoid the vague phrase ``something went wrong''.
\begin{description}
  \item[E1: Failed execution.] The OFT message originating from $q$ on $c_s$ either
        reverts on $c_d$ or expires unredeemed past the lane's nonce timeout.
        Operationalised as: zero $\textsc{OFTReceived}$ event on $c_d$ within the
        protocol-defined message-window for the corresponding $\textsc{OFTSent}$ event on
        $c_s$.
  \item[E2: Abnormal slippage.] The realised execution price on $c_d$ deviates from the
        median quoted price at $t$ by more than a threshold $\tau_{\text{slip}}$ (e.g.\
        50 basis points for stablecoin OFTs, 200 bps for volatile OFTs).
  \item[E3: Bridge / lane delay.] The end-to-end latency between $\textsc{OFTSent}$ on
        $c_s$ and $\textsc{OFTReceived}$ on $c_d$ exceeds a per-lane latency threshold
        $\tau_{\text{delay}}$, calibrated as the historical 99th percentile of that lane.
  \item[E4: Exploit-cluster association.] The destination address, the lane, or the OFT
        is identified within $\Delta'$ blocks (typically $\Delta' \le \Delta$) as
        belonging to an attack cluster by an external label source --- in our setup, the
        \textsc{BridgeGuard} attack-transaction corpus~\cite{wu2025safeguarding}
        (49~incidents, 203~attack transactions) and on-chain forensic feeds.
\end{description}
We define $E_{q, t+\Delta} = 1$ if any of $E1$--$E4$ fires; otherwise $E_{q, t+\Delta} =
0$. The precise channel that fired is preserved as auxiliary metadata so a follow-up
analysis can decompose miner accuracy by event type. \emph{Realised loss} in this paper
always refers to this conjunction of observable channels --- never to ``post-route
drawdown'' or other downstream consequences that depend on a user's holding decision.

\subsection{Material chain-state change}\label{sec:formal:material}

The validator consistency rule (Section~\ref{sec:validator}) compares miner outputs at
nearby times $t$ and $t + \delta$. We define a \emph{material chain-state change} on
route $q$ between $t$ and $t + \delta$ as the disjunction:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item the source-chain or destination-chain head changed by at least one block;
  \item the lane's send-side \texttt{UlnConfig} or \texttt{ExecutorConfig} changed
        (specifically: \texttt{confirmations}, \texttt{requiredDVNCount},
        \texttt{requiredDVNs[]}, \texttt{optionalDVNCount}, \texttt{optionalDVNs[]},
        \texttt{optionalDVNThreshold}, \texttt{maxMessageSize}, or
        \texttt{executor})~\cite{layerzero2024oft,zarick2024layerzero}, or the
        receive-side \texttt{UlnConfig} that the destination endpoint enforces;
  \item liquidity depth on either side of the route changed by more than a fraction
        $\eta$ of pre-trade depth (default $\eta = 0.05$);
  \item the OFT contract on $c_s$ or $c_d$ was upgraded or paused.
\end{enumerate}
If none of these holds between $t$ and $t + \delta$, the chain state is \emph{materially
unchanged} and miner responses are required to be byte-identical (modulo the
quantisation precision of Section~\ref{sec:miner}). If any holds, divergent miner
responses are not penalised on the consistency channel.

The lane-configuration coordinates listed in (2) are the LayerZero V2 surface that
OmniRisk monitors per route. The receive-side \texttt{UlnConfig} is what the destination
endpoint actually enforces, while the send-side configuration determines the verification
path the message will follow~\cite{zarick2024layerzero}; both must be tracked because a
mismatch between them blocks delivery and is itself a risk signal.

\subsection{Confidence calibration}\label{sec:formal:calibration}

A miner's confidence $c_i(q, t)$ is \emph{calibrated} if, over a window of $N$ scored
queries with realised outcomes $y_n \in \{0,1\}$, the empirical Brier score
\begin{equation}
  \text{Brier}_i \;=\; \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^{N} \bigl( s_i(q_n, t_n) - y_n \bigr)^{2}
  \label{eq:brier}
\end{equation}
is no worse than that of a calibrated reference predictor on the same window, and the
self-reported confidence $c_i$ tracks miner accuracy in the sense that the reliability
diagram (binned $c_i$ vs.\ realised accuracy) lies within a tolerance band of the
identity. A miner that reports $c_i = 1$ on every query is automatically miscalibrated
unless its $\text{Brier}_i$ is uniformly zero, which empirically does not occur.

\subsection{Validator loss}\label{sec:formal:vloss}

Let $\mathbf{s}_i, \mathbf{c}_i, \mathbf{y}$ be the miner-$i$ score vector, confidence
vector, and realised-outcome vector over a window of $N$ scored queries. The validator
loss for miner $i$ is the weighted decomposition
\begin{equation}
  L_i \;=\; \alpha \cdot \text{Brier}(\mathbf{s}_i, \mathbf{y})
        \;+\; \beta \cdot \text{Inconsistency}_i
        \;+\; \gamma \cdot \text{Miscalibration}_i
  \label{eq:vloss}
\end{equation}
with non-negative weights $\alpha + \beta + \gamma = 1$ that the subnet publishes as
hyperparameters. The three terms are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\text{Brier}(\mathbf{s}_i, \mathbf{y})$ as in Eq.~\eqref{eq:brier} (realised-event
        accuracy);
  \item $\text{Inconsistency}_i$, the fraction of paired queries $(q, t), (q, t+\delta)$
        with no material chain-state change (Section~\ref{sec:formal:material}) for which
        miner $i$ returned different responses;
  \item $\text{Miscalibration}_i$, the expected calibration error
        $\sum_b w_b \,|\, \overline{c}_b - \overline{a}_b \,|$ over equal-mass bins of
        $c_i$, where $\overline{c}_b$ is the mean confidence in bin $b$ and
        $\overline{a}_b$ is the realised accuracy.
\end{itemize}
A validator transforms its loss vector $\{L_i\}_{i \in M}$ into a Yuma weight vector
$\{W_{ij}\}_{j \in M}$ (using the indexing of Section~\ref{sec:arch:bittensor}, where $i$
now indexes the validator and $j$ indexes the miner). We use the
temperature-scaled-softmax form
\begin{equation}
  W_{ij} \;=\; \frac{\exp(-\lambda \, L_j)}{\sum_{k \in M} \exp(-\lambda \, L_k)}
  \label{eq:vweight}
\end{equation}
with $\lambda > 0$ a subnet-published hyperparameter; any monotone decreasing transform
of $L_j$ suffices, and we use the softmax form for numerical stability. The submitted
weights $W_{ij}$ are then aggregated on chain via Eq.~\eqref{eq:yuma-consensus}: a
miner's emission share $M_j$ is determined by the consensus-clipped
$\overline{W}_{ij}$, not by the validator's pre-clip submission. In particular, a
validator that scores miners outside the $\kappa$-stake-fraction consensus envelope
contributes nothing additional to those miners' emissions and erodes its own bonded
yield --- which is what makes the validator scoring rule of this section economically
self-correcting.

\subsection{Worked example}\label{sec:formal:example}

To make the abstraction concrete, consider a single representative route:
\[
  q^{\star} \;=\; (\,c_s = \text{Arbitrum},\; c_d = \text{Base},\; a = \text{USDC OFT},\;
                   x = 100{,}000\;\text{USDC},\; t \text{ = current finalised heads}\,) \,.
\]
The information set $X_{q^{\star}, t}$ that an OmniRisk-backed miner consults contains:
the source-chain USDC liquidity depth observable on Arbitrum DEXes; the destination-chain
liquidity depth on Base; the lane's send-side \texttt{UlnConfig}
(\texttt{requiredDVNCount}, \texttt{requiredDVNs[]}, \texttt{optionalDVNCount},
\texttt{optionalDVNs[]}, \texttt{optionalDVNThreshold}, \texttt{confirmations}); the
\texttt{ExecutorConfig} (\texttt{maxMessageSize}, \texttt{executor}); the receive-side
\texttt{UlnConfig} on Base; rolling latency and message-failure counts on this lane; and
the BridgeGuard incident-cluster proximity for the destination address and the OFT
contract.

A miner returns $(s_i, c_i, r_i)$. Suppose $s_i = 0.07$ (low risk) and $c_i = 0.85$.
The validator subsequently observes the realised outcome over $[t, t + \Delta]$:
suppose the message commits within the lane's 99th-percentile latency
(no $E3$), executes successfully on Base (no $E1$), at within-50-bp slippage
(no $E2$), and the destination address does not appear in any BridgeGuard cluster
within $\Delta'$ blocks (no $E4$). Then $E_{q^{\star}, t+\Delta} = 0$ and the contribution
to the miner's Brier component is $(0.07 - 0)^2 = 0.0049$. A miner that had instead
emitted $s_i = 0.9$ on the same query would contribute $(0.9 - 0)^2 = 0.81$ --- two
orders of magnitude worse --- and would be ranked correspondingly lower by every
validator that observed the same query, which would clip its consensus weight
$\overline{W}_j$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:yuma-consensus}. The same machinery applies in the
opposite direction: on a query whose realised outcome is $E = 1$, the miner that emitted
$s_i = 0.07$ contributes $0.86$ to its Brier and is clipped down. This is the operational
form of hypotheses H1 and H2 in Section~\ref{sec:rq}.

\section{Miner Protocol Specification}\label{sec:miner}

A miner $i$ is a thin wrapper over the OmniRisk API, exposed at the Bittensor subnet's
miner endpoint. For each route query $q$ as defined in Eq.~\eqref{eq:route}, the miner
returns a tuple $(s_i, c_i, r_i) \in [0,1] \times [0,1] \times \mathcal{R}$, where
$s_i$ is its estimate of the route risk score $s(q,t)$ from Eq.~\eqref{eq:risk}
($s_i = 0$ denoting nominal and $s_i = 1$ denoting an unrouteable lane), $c_i$ is the
miner's self-reported confidence (Section~\ref{sec:formal:calibration}), and $r_i$ is a
structured reasoning record listing which on-chain observations were used, which DEX
quotes were consulted, and which historical incidents from the BridgeGuard
corpus~\cite{wu2025safeguarding} were the closest analogues.

\paragraph{Determinism constraint.} Yuma scoring requires that two miners using the
same internal logic on the same query at the same block height return the same answer.
Otherwise validators cannot meaningfully aggregate them, and good miners are penalised
for irreducible randomness rather than for being wrong. We pin this by (i)~quantising
$s$ and $c$ to a fixed precision (we use 1/256), (ii)~anchoring the miner to a specific
chain head (a 32-byte block hash per chain consulted), and (iii)~specifying the
ordering and version of any external data sources in $r$. This is the same discipline
that makes optimistic-ML rollup systems
~\cite{conway2024opml} workable: outputs must be a deterministic function of the
declared inputs.

\section{Validator Mechanism}\label{sec:validator}

A validator queries a subset of miners on a stratified sample of routes, scores each
response against the realised-event ground truth defined in
Section~\ref{sec:formal:realised}, and submits a Yuma weight vector
$\mathbf{w} \in [0,1]^{N}$ over the $N$ miners it observed. The on-chain Yuma rule then
aggregates $\mathbf{w}$ across all subnet validators using a stake-weighted threshold and
emits rewards~\cite{opentensor2024yuma}.

The scoring rule is the validator loss $L_i$ given in Eq.~\eqref{eq:vloss}. It has three
components, each grounded in a precise definition from Section~\ref{sec:formal}:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Realised-event accuracy} (the $\alpha$ term, Eq.~\eqref{eq:brier}).
        For routes scored between $t$ and $t + \Delta$, the miner's score $s_i$ is
        evaluated against the realised-loss indicator $E_{q, t+\Delta}$
        (Section~\ref{sec:formal:realised}), which is the disjunction of failed
        execution, abnormal slippage, bridge/lane delay, and exploit-cluster association
        from the BridgeGuard corpus~\cite{wu2025safeguarding}. We deliberately exclude
        downstream channels such as ``post-route drawdown'' that would conflate
        route-execution quality with the user's holding decision.
  \item \textbf{Consistency} (the $\beta$ term). If the same query is issued at $t$ and
        $t + \delta$ for small $\delta$ during which the chain state is materially
        unchanged in the precise sense of Section~\ref{sec:formal:material}, the
        responses must be byte-identical (modulo the quantisation precision of
        Section~\ref{sec:miner}). Inconsistency under no material chain-state change
        indicates either non-determinism (already penalised per Section~\ref{sec:miner})
        or unstable internal state.
  \item \textbf{Confidence calibration} (the $\gamma$ term). Calibration is measured by
        the expected calibration error over reliability bins of $c_i$, with the precise
        definition in Section~\ref{sec:formal:calibration}. Miners that report $c_i = 1$
        on every query are automatically miscalibrated, and miners whose self-reported
        confidence tracks realised accuracy receive lower $L_i$ and therefore higher
        Yuma weight, which is the operational form of hypothesis H2
        (Section~\ref{sec:rq}).
\end{enumerate}

The key design property is that validators score the \emph{quality} of intelligence, not
just its presence. Miners that route every score through the same underlying OmniRisk
API but apply different downstream models can still be differentiated, because the
realised-event channel breaks ties.

\section{Cross-Chain Risk Model}\label{sec:risk}

We formalise three risk axes that emerge \emph{between} chains rather than within them:
\begin{description}
  \item[Liquidity fragmentation.] The same token has independent depth on each chain;
        the worst-of distribution dominates a multi-hop route. The empirical
        misallocation documented by~\cite{gogol2024liquidity} for WETH--USDC pools is
        the simplest instance of this; the OFT lane fabric of LayerZero~V2 generalises
        the problem to arbitrary fungible tokens~\cite{layerzero2024oft}.
  \item[Bridge / route dependency risk.] A given OFT lane inherits the security
        configuration of its DVN set and Executor~\cite{zarick2024layerzero}. The
        bridge-security literature catalogues how individual configuration choices map
        onto exploited vulnerability classes
        ~\cite{zhang2023sok,augusto2024sok,wu2025safeguarding}.
  \item[Anomaly propagation.] An exploit on one chain can spread to others via shared
        bridges, shared liquidity, and shared dependencies. Multichain fraud-detection
        datasets~\cite{palaiokrassas2023multichain} provide the empirical basis for
        modelling this, but the cross-chain dimension remains under-instrumented in
        published work.
\end{description}
The unifying observation is that cross-chain risk is \emph{a property of routes, not of
tokens or chains in isolation}, and a verification layer that scores claims about routes
is therefore the natural primitive.

\section{Subtensor EVM (Chain~ID~945) Integration}\label{sec:subtensor}

The Subtensor EVM is an Ethereum-compatible execution environment that runs inside the
Subtensor Substrate runtime, with TAO as the gas asset~\cite{opentensor2024evm}. It is
not just another EVM lane for RayaChain; it is the execution environment whose
validator set is already economically aligned to scoring the quality of off-chain
intelligence, by virtue of the Yuma mechanism~\cite{opentensor2024yuma,
steeves2022incentivizing}. We use it for two distinct purposes:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Anchoring of risk-score commitments.} Each batch of miner outputs is
        committed as a Merkle root on Subtensor EVM, so a downstream contract on any
        of the seven existing RayaChain lanes can fetch a proof that a given
        $(s, c, r)$ tuple was produced and made available at a specific block height,
        prior to the route being taken.
  \item \textbf{Anchoring of validator-consensus outputs.} The aggregated Yuma reward
        vector for the cross-chain risk subnet is itself published on Subtensor EVM,
        which gives a downstream auditor a single canonical record of which miners
        were trusted at any given epoch.
\end{enumerate}
This aligns the economic incentives of the AI layer (miners and validators), the
liquidity layer (RayaChain LPs), and the verification layer (TAO holders staking on
the subnet).

\section{Data and Evaluation Framework}\label{sec:eval}

The evaluation we are building rests on four datasets. As of submission-draft date,
all of them are restricted to \emph{testnet} flows: RayaChain has zero mainnet
deployments (Section~\ref{sec:status:gaps}), so the pre-mainnet evaluation must be
read as a methodological dry-run rather than as evidence about production cross-chain
risk. The datasets are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{RayaChain flow logs (testnet).} \texttt{OFTSent} /
        \texttt{OFTReceived} events on the six registered testnet lanes
        (Ethereum/Arbitrum/Base Sepolia, BSC testnet, Polygon Amoy, Solana
        devnet), with per-lane metadata (DVN set, Executor, observed latencies,
        message-failure counts). Two additional caveats apply pre-mainnet:
        (a)~only 3 of 20 directional EVM$\leftrightarrow$EVM peer routes are
        configured (Section~\ref{sec:status:gaps}), so the cross-chain sample
        is dominated by a handful of routes and the empirical distribution is
        not representative; (b)~until the Transfer Engine ships persistence,
        records have to be reconstructed by joining \texttt{OFTSent} on source
        against \texttt{OFTReceived} on destination per LayerZero~V2 GUID,
        and the evaluation pipeline must record and report the join failure
        rate. Mainnet flows will replace this dataset as the primary basis
        for any quantitative claim, in a follow-up paper, once mainnet cuts
        over.
  \item \textbf{Route Health time series.} The 15-second-cadence p50/p99 latency,
        destination liquidity, recent volume, success rate, and risk-grade telemetry
        published in production by the Route Health System
        (Section~\ref{sec:arch:rayachain}). This dataset is the input to the
        cross-chain features of any miner that needs more than a single-event
        snapshot, and is the natural ground truth for the liquidity-health
        component of the route-risk score $s(q,t)$.
  \item \textbf{Realised-event labels.} A union of (a)~slippage and
        execution-failure outcomes derived from the flow logs and the Route Health
        success-rate channel (the $E_1$--$E_3$ channels of
        Section~\ref{sec:formal:realised}) and (b)~external incident labels from
        peer-reviewed and grey-literature corpora (the $E_4$ channel); the
        BridgeGuard dataset~\cite{wu2025safeguarding} (203 attack transactions
        across 49~incidents) is one of the curated bases.
  \item \textbf{Replay routes.} For each historical route observed in~(1), we
        replay it to the OmniRisk-backed miners under the configuration at the
        corresponding block height (Section~\ref{sec:miner}), and score against
        (3). Until the Transfer Engine ships persistence, the replay set is
        restricted to routes whose source/destination events successfully joined
        in~(1).
\end{enumerate}
The headline metrics are realised-event ROC-AUC, precision-recall AUC (the latter is
required because the BridgeGuard prior gives a class-imbalance ratio of approximately
$1{:}197$ for $E4$ events and ROC-AUC alone is misleading at that imbalance,
cf.~Section~\ref{sec:related:bridge-detection}), early-detection lead time (how many
blocks before an event the score crossed threshold), expected calibration error
(Section~\ref{sec:formal:calibration}), and Yuma reward retention (a miner's reward share
over a window normalised by its scored share, as a measure of alignment with the
validator consensus, computed against the consensus weight $\overline{W}_j$ of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:yuma-consensus}).

\subsection{Baselines}\label{sec:eval:baselines}

A claim that a verifiable cross-chain risk primitive outperforms the status quo is only
defensible against explicit baselines. Each of the following is implemented as a
\emph{reference miner} on the same subnet, scored under the same validator loss
$L_i$ as any production miner, and reported alongside the OmniRisk miner in every
follow-up evaluation. They span the design space from naive to credible analytic
alternatives.

\begin{description}
  \item[B1: Random / naive baseline.] Returns $s = 0.5$ on every query, with
        $c = 0.5$. This is the lower bound: any miner whose Brier score does not improve
        on B1 is providing no information.
  \item[B2: Single-chain liquidity-only model.] A logistic regressor over destination-chain
        liquidity depth, recent destination-chain volatility, and asset identifier ---
        \emph{no source-chain or lane features}. This baseline isolates the value of
        cross-chain telemetry; rejection of H1 (Section~\ref{sec:rq}) corresponds to the
        OmniRisk miner failing to improve early-detection lead time over B2.
  \item[B3: Static bridge-risk heuristic.] A fixed mapping from
        $(c_s, c_d, \text{DVN set}, \text{Executor})$ to a constant risk tier, calibrated
        once at evaluation start and never updated. This baseline isolates the value of
        the agentic, adaptive component of OmniRisk against a static analyst-tier
        product.
  \item[B4: Generic anomaly detector.] A standard isolation-forest or one-class SVM
        trained on the union of source- and destination-chain features but without any
        bridge-/lane-specific structure or the BridgeGuard incident corpus. This baseline
        isolates the value of incident-grounded supervision against unsupervised
        anomaly scoring.
\end{description}

For each baseline we report (i)~Brier score, (ii)~realised-event AUC, (iii)~early-detection
lead time, (iv)~expected calibration error, and (v)~Yuma reward retention over the
replay window. The OmniRisk miner is required to dominate B1 strictly on all five
metrics, dominate B2 on (iii) for hypothesis H1 to survive, and dominate B3 on (i)--(iii)
for the agentic-vs-static claim of Section~\ref{sec:compare} to be defensible.

\section{Case Study: The RYA Defence Cycle, 2026-05-06}\label{sec:case-study}

The five-engine architecture of Section~\ref{sec:five-engine} is, at the time of
writing, partly deployed and partly a design proposal. To ground the prose in
production data, this section instruments a single fully-public five-hour run of the
\emph{scenario engine} (Section~\ref{sec:engine:scenario}) operating on a single
Solana micro-cap pool: the \textsc{RYA} buy-only liquidity-defence cycle of
2026-05-06. The cycle is an honest single-instance demonstration --- not a
statistical claim --- of what the engines look like in production when the agentic
engine is instantiated as a deterministic policy.

The companion operator-side narrative is published
publicly~\cite{omnirisk2026defending}; this section reports the timestamps,
prices, slice geometry, and post-cycle state directly from the bot's decision log,
a tracked file under the OmniRisk operations repository. The bot's full source code
and configuration knobs (other than the deployer/RPC secrets) are public.

\subsection{Setup}\label{sec:case-study:setup}

\paragraph{Asset and venue.} \textsc{RYA} is the RayaChain protocol token
(Section~\ref{sec:economics:rya:design}); the Solana mint is
\texttt{Eebqv\dots susV} (Solana Program Library token), traded on a single Raydium
constant-product (CPMM) pool launched 2026-05-05 with 37~SOL of initial liquidity.
The pool is the scenario engine's sole execution venue for this cycle; routing is
through Jupiter, on the grounds that Jupiter is the standard Solana DEX router and
that a single-venue execution path is simpler to instrument.

\paragraph{Wallet and policy.} The defence wallet is a non-treasury hot wallet
\texttt{AHbBv\dots EX3} funded explicitly outside the treasury / Squads / deployer
key custody. Hard rules from the Trader role contract: \emph{(i)~buy-only --- no sell
path is implemented, (ii)~the deployer wallet is never used, (iii)~live mode requires
\texttt{DRY\_RUN=false}, an explicit \texttt{LIVE\_CONFIRM} acknowledgement, and a
board sign-off recorded in the issue thread, (iv)~daily-cap raises require a written
rationale and a CEO approval, (v)~no human impersonation; the wallet is one address
and any anti-MEV jitter is anti-MEV only}. These rules are the agentic engine's
constitution under the Trader role
(Sections~\ref{sec:engine:agentic},~\ref{sec:arch:omnirisk}).

\paragraph{Tier ladder and caps.} The scenario engine reads its tier ladder, daily
cap, per-trade ceiling, cooldown, TWAP slicing, and ratchet bounds from the bot's
\texttt{.env} file. As of the start of the 2026-05-06 cycle (post the cap raise
recorded at 01:07~UTC, see Section~\ref{sec:case-study:timeline}):
\texttt{MAX\_DAILY\_SOL}~$=$~3.0, \texttt{MAX\_TRADE\_SOL}~$=$~0.10,
\texttt{COOLDOWN\_SECONDS}~$=$~600, \texttt{TWAP\_SLICES}~$=$~5,
\texttt{TWAP\_DURATION\_SECONDS}~$=$~30, \texttt{SLIPPAGE\_BPS}~$=$~150,

\texttt{SUPPORT\_TIERS}~$=$~\texttt{0.00016:0.05, 0.00013:0.07, 0.00011:0.08,

(in SOL); the bottom tier --- \texttt{0.00009 USD : 0.10 SOL} --- is the one

\texttt{RATCHET\_TRIGGER\_SOL}~$=$~2.0~SOL of cumulative spend, capped at
\texttt{RATCHET\_MAX\_PRICE\_USD}~$=$~0.00020.

\subsection{Reading the cycle as a five-engine instance}\label{sec:case-study:engines}

This single cycle is an explicit instance of the architecture of
Section~\ref{sec:five-engine}, with the agentic and prediction engines
deliberately downgraded for legibility:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The \emph{prediction engine} is reduced to a single rule: ``the price is
        below the bottom tier of the support ladder''. No distributional forecast.
        The simplification is intentional --- it is the smallest non-trivial
        prediction primitive --- and the case study is correspondingly cautious about
        the strength of the evidence it provides.
  \item The \emph{Bittensor prediction subnet} is not active in this cycle. The
        prediction primitive is a single deterministic predicate evaluated by the
        bot. The integration of the live subnet is the next-step build.
  \item The \emph{sentiment-fusion engine} is consulted only on the cycle-entry
        decision: the 60.9\% / 24h drawdown, the 24.2\% / 6h drawdown, the 6:15
        buy/sell asymmetry over six hours, and the public attention surface on
        Birdeye/DexScreener constitute the adverse-sentiment evidence that forced
        the cycle-entry call. After cycle-entry, sentiment is not re-consulted; the
        cycle is run open-loop on the price tier ladder until the daily cap binds.
  \item The \emph{agentic engine} is instantiated as a deterministic Trader-role
        policy under the role-contract constitution of
        Section~\ref{sec:case-study:setup}. The cycle-entry decision (the live
        flip and the cap raise) is exactly the human-approval boundary the agentic
        engine of Section~\ref{sec:engine:agentic} requires; the cycle itself is
        the rule-based action surface that the role contract authorised.
  \item The \emph{scenario engine} is the part of the architecture that is most
        clearly running in production: the program (``buy at the bottom tier on a
        TWAP geometry until the daily cap binds''), the trigger (``price below
        bottom tier and cooldown elapsed''), the action (``execute five
        $\times$~0.02 SOL Jupiter buys at 30~s spacing''), and the post-condition
        (``daily cap binds; cycle ends'') are the four typed fields of the
        scenario-program record of Section~\ref{sec:arch:scenario}. The full
        cycle is a single program instance.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{What we are not claiming.} We make no causal claim that the bot's buys
caused the price recovery; the recovery is concurrent and the natural-experiment
counterfactual is unavailable. We make no statistical claim from a single 5-hour
cycle on a single pool. We make no claim that the rule-based scenario engine of
this cycle is what the LLM-mediated agentic engine of Section~\ref{sec:engine:agentic}
will look like in production; that is the next-step build. The case study's
purpose is to demonstrate that the program-registry / trigger-evaluator /
constrained-execution decomposition is sufficient to instrument a real defensive
action against a Solana micro-cap pool dump in the threat regime
of~\cite{cernera2023token}, with public timestamps and signatures.

\section{Economic Model and Tokenomics}\label{sec:economics}

The architecture has two distinct economic surfaces: a verification-side surface that
rewards Bittensor miners and validators for accurate cross-chain risk outputs
(Section~\ref{sec:economics:bittensor}), and an application-side surface in which
the Rayachain protocol token, \textsc{RYA}, captures the protocol's product revenue
and aligns long-term participation with the value of the decentralised
risk-intelligence network
(Sections~\ref{sec:economics:rya:design}--\ref{sec:economics:rya:bittensor}). The two
surfaces are intentionally separated: TAO emissions reward verification work, RYA
flows reward consumption and distribution work, and a designed bridge between the
two routes a portion of RYA-denominated fee revenue back to the Bittensor subnet
participants whose signals drive the Rayachain broadcasts
(Section~\ref{sec:economics:rya:bittensor}).

\subsection{Bittensor verification surface}\label{sec:economics:bittensor}

On the verification side, the economic flows are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Miners are rewarded in TAO via Yuma emissions for risk outputs that the
        validator consensus accepts~\cite{opentensor2024yuma,steeves2022incentivizing}.
  \item Validators are rewarded for assigning weights that align with the consensus,
        which is the natural Yuma incentive to score accurately.
  \item OmniRisk is the infrastructure provider: it operates the underlying analytics
        platform that miners wrap, and it monetises this through its existing API
        surface to non-Bittensor consumers.
\end{itemize}
We avoid claims about token-economic equilibria here. The Bittensor critical-analysis
literature~\cite{lui2025bittensor} documents real concerns about Bittensor's
centralisation properties under dynamic TAO, and any quantitative economic claim about
this subnet should be revisited against that work in a follow-up.

\subsection{The RYA token: design rationale}\label{sec:economics:rya:design}

\textsc{RYA} is the Rayachain protocol token. It is specified, at this stage, as a
fixed-supply asset with no protocol mint authority post-launch and a fee-capture
model in which a designed fraction of every protocol fee is converted to RYA on the
open market and distributed to stakers. Three design decisions follow from the
project's institutional-acceptance and verifiability constraints, and we state them
explicitly because each one removes a degree of freedom that bridge-token designs
typically retain.

\paragraph{Fixed supply.} Total supply is capped at $10^9$~RYA at the
token-generation event (TGE) and is pre-minted in full at TGE. There is no
protocol-level inflation lever after launch. This rules out an entire class of
governance-driven supply expansions that downstream consumers of risk telemetry
would otherwise have to discount.

\paragraph{No protocol-level mint path post-TGE.} The end-state guarantee --- post-TGE
no signer can expand supply --- holds on every chain, with chain-specific mechanics.
On Solana, the mint authority is revoked via \texttt{setAuthority(mint, null)} in the
same operational window as TGE and initial liquidity-pool initialisation, and the
freeze authority is \texttt{null} from the mint instruction itself, so the token is
irreversibly non-mintable and non-freezable. On each EVM spoke, ownership of the OFT
contract is retained at the 3-of-5 Safe multisig --- not burned --- because LayerZero
peer management, endpoint and DVN configuration, and Guardian assignment must remain
callable for the lifetime of the protocol; supply expansion is instead removed at
the code level, with no \texttt{mint()} function in production bytecode and no
owner-gated \texttt{\_mint} path. A separate Guardian role is restricted to
\texttt{pause}/\texttt{unpause} of OFT bridging and cannot mint, transfer, change
ownership, or upgrade. The intrinsic OFT cross-chain mechanic --- burn-on-source and
mint-on-destination during a transfer --- conserves total supply across all six
chains. \emph{No subsequent mint or freeze is possible at the protocol level on any
chain.} We use the formulation ``no protocol mint path post-TGE'' rather than the
softer ``may be revoked later'' because verifiable-by-construction is a stronger
property than governance-revocable: a downstream contract can read the absence of a
mint authority (Solana) or the absence of any mint function in the deployed
bytecode (EVM) rather than trust that governance will not exercise it.

\paragraph{Pre-minted, lockup-contract emissions.} The 35\% emissions allocation
(Section~\ref{sec:economics:rya:emissions}) is not minted on a discretionary
schedule; it is pre-minted at TGE and parked in an on-chain lockup contract whose
release schedule is published and immutable post-deployment. Because no
protocol-level mint path exists post-TGE, no other emission path exists. This eliminates the
discretionary-minting degree of freedom that would otherwise let a future governance
decision accelerate or expand emissions.

The cumulative effect is a token whose supply trajectory is a published function of
time from TGE forward, and whose total cap is not subsequently revisable by any
party including the founding team. We argue this is the configuration most
consistent with the verifiability claim that motivates the rest of this paper: the
verification layer is decentralised, the supply schedule is deterministic, and the
on-chain configuration of both is the source of truth.

\subsection{Emissions}\label{sec:economics:rya:emissions}

The 35\% emissions bucket (350{,}000{,}000~RYA) is the largest single allocation
and is the mechanism by which protocol participation is bootstrapped without a
pre-TGE investor round. It is pre-minted at TGE to an on-chain lockup contract;
because no protocol-level mint path exists post-TGE
(Section~\ref{sec:economics:rya:design}), no further emission path exists.

\paragraph{Curve shape.} The emission curve is front-loaded with gradual reduction
over a 4--5-year horizon. Two viable shapes are under consideration; the final shape
is locked in a separate emissions-schedule child issue and is not asserted by this
paper:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Yearly decay.} Approximately 30\% reduction per year, so the
        annual weights of the 35\% bucket are roughly $0.30 / 0.22 / 0.16 / 0.12 /
        0.09$ for years 1--5.
  \item \textbf{Halving epochs.} An annual halving schedule, in which year~1 emits
        approximately 50\% of the bucket and each subsequent year halves; the tail
        decays to dust by year~5--6.
\end{itemize}
Either shape is gradual reduction without cliff drops; the lockup contract is
immutable post-deployment, so the curve cannot be rescheduled or accelerated by
governance.

\paragraph{Allocation.} The emissions bucket is split across three reward channels.
\emph{Stakers} are the primary recipients: RYA stakers earn emission rewards in
proportion to stake weight and lockup duration (the staking primitive is specified
in Section~\ref{sec:economics:rya:utility}). \emph{Contributors and ecosystem
participants} receive a share for bug bounties, integration grants, data-quality
incentives for OmniRisk consumers, and dispute-resolution work on the
risk-broadcast layer. A reserve carve-out is allocated to \emph{Bittensor-aligned
subnet participants} producing the AI risk signals that drive Rayachain broadcasts
(Section~\ref{sec:economics:rya:bittensor}).

\subsection{Fee model}\label{sec:economics:rya:fees}

RYA captures real protocol cash flow rather than relying on emission alone for
holder yield. The fee model has three revenue sources and a single distribution rule
(Figure~\ref{fig:rya-fee-flow}).

\paragraph{Revenue sources.} (i)~Cross-chain transfer fees on RYA itself and on
partner-token transfers routed through the Rayachain OFT lanes; (ii)~per-broadcast
fees on the Risk Broadcast System (Section~\ref{sec:arch:rayachain}) for cross-chain
risk-score and verdict publication; (iii)~subscription revenue from institutional
and retail consumers of the OmniRisk risk-intelligence API.

\paragraph{Fee flow.} Users pay protocol fees in USDC or chain-native gas tokens
--- \emph{they are not required to hold RYA to consume the product}. A tunable
fraction of every fee, denoted $\phi \in [0.30, 0.50]$, is converted to RYA via
market-buy on the canonical DEX pools. The bought RYA is distributed to stakers as
a fee-yield reward stream that is independent of, and additive to, the emission
rewards of Section~\ref{sec:economics:rya:emissions}. The remaining $1 - \phi$
flows to the treasury for operations, ongoing audits, market-maker partnerships,
grants, and runway. The buy-back ratio $\phi$ is multisig-tunable within the
$[0.30, 0.50]$ band and is moved out of band only by DAO vote once governance ships.

The structural property that follows from this design is that real product
consumption produces real RYA buy pressure on a public DEX, and the resulting yield

$\phi > 0$, every unit of fee revenue is an upper bound on the RYA inflow to
stakers in that period, regardless of governance.

\subsection{Token utility}\label{sec:economics:rya:utility}

RYA serves three product roles and is designed so that holding RYA is rewarded by
all three simultaneously:
\begin{description}
  \item[Staking with a dual reward stream.] Locking RYA earns (a)~emission rewards
        from the Section~\ref{sec:economics:rya:emissions} bucket and (b)~fee-yield
        rewards from the Section~\ref{sec:economics:rya:fees} buy-back. Lockup
        duration weights the staker's share of both streams; longer lockups earn
        more.
  \item[Fee discounts.] Stakers and qualifying holders pay reduced rates on
        broadcast and API fees. The discount is denominated in the same fee
        currency the user already pays, so the discount surface does not by itself
        impose a requirement to hold RYA.
  \item[Access tiers.] Stake size unlocks priority risk-scoring, higher API rate
        limits, and access to premium broadcast lanes. The tiers are a
        product-side gate, not a regulatory one.
\end{description}
The economic intent of these three together is that RYA is simultaneously a
cash-flow-bearing asset (via the staker dual-reward stream), a cost-basis
instrument for power users (via the discount), and an access credential gating
premium product surfaces (via the tiers). None of the three relies on speculative
supply growth: the cap is fixed and emission is on a published curve.

\subsection{Bittensor value-flow alignment}\label{sec:economics:rya:bittensor}

RYA is the \emph{distribution layer} for Rayachain risk broadcasts that are, in
turn, downstream consumers of Bittensor subnet outputs. RYA is not a
Subtensor-native asset --- there is no SPL or EIP RYA mint on Subtensor, and the
verification-layer incentives of Section~\ref{sec:economics:bittensor} continue to
be denominated in TAO. The integration between RYA economics and the Bittensor
subnet is at the value-flow layer, with two concrete present-day levers and one
explicitly forward-compatible governance lever.

\paragraph{Fee routing to subnet contributors.} A portion of fee revenue from risk
broadcasts that consume Bittensor subnet outputs is routed to the subnet
participants and contributors producing those signals. This lever is implementable
on the buy-back router (Section~\ref{sec:economics:rya:fees}) and does not depend
on any subnet-governance modification.

\paragraph{Distribution carve-out.} The reserve carve-out within the
Section~\ref{sec:economics:rya:emissions} emissions bucket allocates RYA to subnet
operators delivering quality risk-aligned outputs.

\paragraph{Forward-compatible governance lever.} A future state in which staked
RYA influences Bittensor subnet model selection or weighting --- so that RYA
stakers vote on which subnet outputs are accepted into Rayachain broadcasts and
what weight each subnet's signals carry --- is consistent with the design but is
\emph{not} a technical commitment of this paper. We flag it as a forward-compatible
governance hook because the underlying validator-loss machinery
(Section~\ref{sec:formal:vloss}) and the score-commitment surface
(Section~\ref{sec:subtensor}) are the components a later subnet-governance design
would build on, but neither is required to ship in the initial mainnet
configuration.

The cumulative positioning is that RYA is the access and (eventually) governance
asset over a Bittensor-aligned cross-chain risk-intelligence network, with current
flows already routing real fee revenue to subnet contributors and a defined,
non-discretionary path to staking-influences-weighting governance once the
Section~\ref{sec:subtensor} commitments are deployed.

\section{Security Model}\label{sec:security}

The threat surface is dominated by three classes:
\begin{description}
  \item[Adversarial miners.] A miner can attempt to bias the risk score it emits, for
        example to suppress a warning on a lane it benefits from. The Yuma scoring rule
        bounds the influence of such a miner to its share of stake-weighted
        rank~\cite{steeves2022incentivizing}; the determinism and consistency
        components of the validator rule (Section~\ref{sec:validator}) further limit
        steady-state bias.
  \item[Manipulated liquidity signals.] An attacker can briefly distort the on-chain
        signals that miners ingest --- e.g.\ wash-trading a thin pool to mask an exit.
        Our mitigation is multi-source data validation: the validator's
        realised-event channel (Section~\ref{sec:validator}) is by construction
        retrospective and ignores manipulated quote-time signals.
  \item[Cross-chain spoofing.] A malicious bridge can claim a state on one chain that
        is not consistent with the source. The OFT design~\cite{layerzero2024oft}
        and the V2 DVN model~\cite{zarick2024layerzero} reduce, but do not eliminate,
        this risk; the bridge-security literature documents the residual surface in
        depth~\cite{zhang2023sok,augusto2024sok,wu2025safeguarding}.
\end{description}

\subsection{Known production gaps}\label{sec:status:gaps}

The following gaps were surfaced by two consecutive engineering audits on
2026-05-03 --- a RayaChain system summary describing the runtime modules and an
independent mainnet-readiness audit of the \texttt{rayachain-oft} repository ---
and are recorded here so that no claim in this paper depends on them being
closed. Each item is concrete and falsifiable; each should be verified against
a fresh audit immediately before submission.

\paragraph{Mainnet readiness (mainnet-readiness audit, 2026-05-03).} These
items must close before any mainnet claim is defensible.

\begin{description}
  \item[Zero mainnet deployments.] The \texttt{rayachain-oft} repository
        contains no mainnet deployment artefacts: no mainnet program ID, no
        mainnet keypair, no mainnet OFT contract on any of the five EVM
        chains, and no mainnet entry in the registry. Estimated time to
        mainnet-ready (engineering): four to eight weeks.
  \item[Canonical origin pointer is testnet.] The registry's canonical
        origin is \texttt{solana-devnet} (LayerZero V2 EID 40168). It must be
        repointed to a Solana mainnet endpoint (and a corresponding registry
        entry created) before launch.
  \item[Hardhat config has no mainnet networks.] All five EVM networks in
        \texttt{chain/evm/hardhat.config.ts} use testnet EIDs
        (\texttt{SEPOLIA\_V2\_TESTNET}, \texttt{ARBSEP\_V2\_TESTNET},
        \texttt{BASESEP\_V2\_TESTNET}, \texttt{BSC\_V2\_TESTNET},
        \texttt{AMOY\_V2\_TESTNET}). Mainnet RPC URLs and mainnet EIDs are
        not yet declared.
  \item[Peer configuration is empty across the board.] The on-chain peer
        verification artefact (\texttt{peer-verification.generated.json},
        2026-03-27) records 20 directional EVM$\leftrightarrow$EVM peer
        checks. Only three pass: Arbitrum~Sepolia~$\to$~Base~Sepolia,
        Arbitrum~Sepolia~$\to$~Ethereum~Sepolia, and
        Ethereum~Sepolia~$\to$~Arbitrum~Sepolia. The other 17 directions
        return \texttt{NoPeer: on-chain peer is zero} --- any cross-chain
        send on those routes will revert with \texttt{NoPeer(uint32)}. The
        broadcast-readiness artefact records \texttt{peerConfigured: false}
        for every chain.
  \item[Base Sepolia ownership mismatch.] The Base Sepolia OFT owner is
        \texttt{0x958f\dots} but the deployer key is \texttt{0xE60c\dots}.
        This mismatch (documented in the repository's
        \texttt{docs/raya-evm-oft-owner-audit.md}) currently blocks Base
        peer wiring and must be resolved before the Base Sepolia
        $\leftrightarrow$ peer set can be brought up.
  \item[No mainnet governance.] No multisig, no timelock, and no documented
        key-rotation plan. Mainnet ownership of OFT contracts must move
        from a deployer EOA to (at minimum) a 3-of-5 multisig with a
        timelock on peer updates and supply-affecting calls.
  \item[No third-party security audit.] Neither \texttt{RayaChainOFT.sol}
        nor the Solana program has a third-party audit on record. A
        mainnet launch without one is the failure mode the bridge-security
        SoK literature most consistently documents
        \cite{zhang2023sok,augusto2024sok}.
  \item[No mainnet runbook.] Only testnet deploy runbooks
        (\texttt{docs/runbooks/solana-devnet-deploy.md}) exist. A mainnet
        runbook covering gas, confirmation thresholds, peer-wiring order,
        and rollback procedure has to be written and rehearsed before
        cutover.
\end{description}

\paragraph{Operational state (system summary, 2026-05-03).} These items are
known production gaps in the testnet integration itself.

\begin{description}
  \item[Transfer persistence.] The Transfer Engine does not yet persist
        in-flight or settled transfer records. This is flagged by the
        operator as a critical gap. Until it ships, the realised-loss labels
        $E_1$--$E_3$ of Section~\ref{sec:formal:realised} have to be
        reconstructed from on-chain events rather than from a transfer
        store, which is feasible but introduces a join the evaluation
        framework (Section~\ref{sec:eval}) must explicitly handle.
  \item[Slippage controls.] User-side slippage handling in the Transfer
        Engine is not yet implemented. The risk-score consumer assumed by
        Section~\ref{sec:integration} can therefore advise but cannot
        \emph{enforce} that transfers below a threshold $\theta$ revert; a
        downstream contract that wants enforcement must implement it
        itself today.
  \item[Risk Broadcast not yet operational.] The broadcast readiness flag
        is currently \texttt{false}: the path is wired but sending is
        disabled pending (a)~the executor sidecar implementation for the
        Solana leg and (b)~a TTL mechanism on the broadcast store to
        prevent unbounded growth. The verification-architecture argument
        of Section~\ref{sec:integration} does not depend on broadcast
        being live, but a production deployment of the subnet design
        does.
  \item[Unverified EVM~$\leftrightarrow$~EVM routes.] On testnet, only 3 of
        20 directional EVM$\leftrightarrow$EVM routes pass peer
        verification (see the mainnet-readiness paragraph above). The
        OmniRisk score for any unverified route should be treated as a
        lower bound on real risk until the peer set is wired.
  \item[Decimal handling under registry miss.] Asset-decimal handling is
        unsafe when the registry lookup is a miss; the registry fallback
        path is itself flagged as inconsistent. This is an operational
        correctness issue rather than a security one, but it is upstream
        of the realised-loss channel $E_1$ (slippage), so the
        evaluation framework should filter on registry-resolved
        transfers when computing the $E_1$ contribution.
  \item[API surface drift.] Of the 24~implemented HTTP endpoints (the
        Transfers, Broadcast, and Infrastructure categories of the system
        summary), the operator reports that some handlers exist but are
        not yet wired into the public router. This does not affect any
        result in this paper, but consumers of the evaluation framework
        should treat undocumented endpoints as out-of-scope.
\end{description}

\paragraph{Security incident (mainnet-readiness audit, 2026-05-03).}
Recorded here for completeness; the items themselves are tracked outside
this paper.

\begin{description}
  \item[Plaintext deployer keys committed to a tracked file.] Three plaintext
        private-key entries in \texttt{.env}
        (\texttt{EVM\_DEPLOYER\_PRIVATE\_KEY},
        \texttt{EVM\_PRIVATE\_KEY}, \texttt{BASE\_OFT\_OWNER\_PK}) all hold
        the same secret, controlling the deployer EOA
        \texttt{0xE60c37E3db66d78a79A81097a1942cdf74a9c9Fc}. The key must

  \item[Plaintext Alchemy API key.] The same file exposes an Alchemy API

        \texttt{ETHEREUM\_SEPOLIA\_RPC\_URL}. This must be rotated and

\end{description}

These gaps do not undermine the verification-architecture thesis: the

this paper to testnet-derived measurements until mainnet cuts over.

\section{Comparative Advantage}\label{sec:compare}

Existing cross-chain risk tooling is some combination of single-chain, static, or
black-box. The architecture we propose is differentiated on three axes simultaneously:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Agentic, not static.} The intelligence layer adapts its tool use to the
        question, rather than precomputing a fixed risk surface.
  \item \textbf{Cross-chain-native, not single-chain.} Routes, not tokens, are the
        primitive being scored, which is the level at which fragmentation and
        bridge-dependency risk actually emerge~\cite{gogol2024liquidity,
        zhang2023sok}.
  \item \textbf{Verifiable, not black-box.} The Bittensor verification layer makes
        miner outputs economically expensive to bias, and the on-chain commitments on
        Subtensor EVM (Section~\ref{sec:subtensor}) make their history auditable from
        any of the other RayaChain lanes.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}

We explicitly flag the following limitations.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{The case study is a single 5-hour cycle on a single Solana
        micro-cap pool.} Section~\ref{sec:case-study} is a single-instance
        demonstration of the scenario-engine decomposition, not a statistical
        claim. We do not assert that the bot caused the price recovery; we do not
        assert that the cycle would replicate on a different pool, on a different
        venue, or at a different liquidity scale. The natural-experiment
        counterfactual (``what would have happened without the bot?'') is
        unavailable. The cycle's purpose is to instantiate the program-registry /
        trigger-evaluator / constrained-execution decomposition with public
        timestamps and signatures.
  \item \textbf{The agentic engine in the case study is rule-based.} The cycle in
        Section~\ref{sec:case-study} runs the agentic engine as a deterministic
        Trader-role policy. The LLM-mediated agentic engine of
        Section~\ref{sec:engine:agentic} --- a constitutional-AI-style decision
        layer in the sense of \cite{bai2022constitutional} --- is the next-step
        build, sequenced after the live Bittensor subnet integration of
        Section~\ref{sec:engine:subnet}. The rule-based instantiation is
        sufficient to demonstrate the scenario-engine decomposition but does not
        validate the agentic engine itself.
  \item \textbf{Most quantitative claims are deferred.} The score-commitment
        contracts, the Bittensor subnet, and the evaluation framework
        (Section~\ref{sec:eval}) are design proposals; this paper does not yet report
        AUC numbers, reward-retention curves, or measured comparisons against the
        baselines of Section~\ref{sec:eval:baselines}. The hypotheses H1 and H2
        (Section~\ref{sec:rq}) are stated as falsifiable claims, not as established
        results.
  \item \textbf{Bittensor centralisation risk.} Independent empirical work has raised
        concerns about Bittensor's actual decentralisation under the dynamic-TAO
        regime~\cite{lui2025bittensor}. We treat the Yuma collusion-resistance bound
        of \cite{steeves2022incentivizing} as a \emph{design property of the algorithm}
        rather than as a guarantee about the deployed network's stake distribution.
        The same caveat applies to the prediction-subnet claim of
        Section~\ref{sec:engine:subnet}: a TAO-incentivised distributed prediction
        subnet is collusion-resistant under the Yuma bound, but its empirical
        decentralisation has to be revisited against the deployed stake distribution.
  \item \textbf{Withdrawn primary source.} The original BitTensor preprint
        (arXiv:2003.03917) was withdrawn by its authors as obsolete on 2021-11-10. Any
        prior whitepaper that cited it should be reread against the canonical
        \cite{rao2021bittensor} or the workshop
        formalisation~\cite{steeves2022incentivizing}.
  \item \textbf{Determinism in agentic LLM components.} Section~\ref{sec:miner}'s
        determinism contract is straightforward for classical analytics but is more
        delicate when the miner uses an LLM under the hood; the
        consistency component of the validator rule
        (Section~\ref{sec:validator}) is what catches the residual non-determinism in
        practice.
  \item \textbf{Grey-literature feeds in the sentiment-fusion engine.} The Solana
        sentiment modality of Section~\ref{sec:arch:sentiment} relies on
        non-peer-reviewed dashboards (Birdeye, DexScreener) and meme-coin launch
        trackers (Pump.fun). These are functionally part of the attention surface
        but are operationally fragile; the fusion engine must continue to function
        when any single grey-literature feed degrades or is rate-limited.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

Cross-chain risk has settled into a regime where the dominant losses come from a small
number of well-understood failure modes in the bridge layer, but where the risk
\emph{intelligence} that participants need is itself untrusted. We have presented an
architecture that splits intelligence (OmniRisk), data and execution (RayaChain on
LayerZero~V2), and verification (Bittensor's Yuma Consensus, anchored on Subtensor EVM
chain~ID~945) into three layers with clean economic boundaries. The result, when fully
deployed, is the first production cross-chain risk primitive whose outputs are
verifiable in the same sense that the underlying transfers are: by an independent,
economically aligned, decentralised process. The intermediate deliverables on the
roadmap (Section~\ref{sec:roadmap}) are testable in isolation, and we will report on
each in follow-up work.
